# Camouflage & concealment

> **Disclaimer:** Educational survival/defensive fieldcraft — how to avoid being
> seen, for protection. Not instructions for setting ambushes or harming anyone.

In an era of pervasive drones, thermal sensors, and overhead surveillance, *not being
seen* is the central survival skill. Camouflage breaks up what a watcher (or a sensor)
is looking for; concealment puts terrain between you and the observer; cover stops
fragments and rounds. Aim for all three.

## The signature checklist — shape, shine, shadow, silhouette, spacing, movement

- **Shape** — the human outline is unmistakable. Break it up with vegetation, nets,
  and irregular patterns; avoid hard straight edges.
- **Shine** — skin, glass (optics, phone screens), watches, and wet gear reflect.
  Cover or dull reflective surfaces; keep screens off or shielded.
- **Shadow** — stay *in* shadow, and be aware your own cast shadow can reveal you,
  especially from above at low sun angles.
- **Silhouette** — never skyline yourself on ridgelines or in doorways/windows; keep
  a backdrop behind you.
- **Spacing** — disperse. Clusters of people read instantly to an observer and make a
  single munition lucrative. Modern conflicts punish bunching.
- **Movement** — the eye and the sensor catch motion first. Move slow, low, and at
  limited visibility; stop fully when you hear/see a drone.

## Thermal & multispectral awareness

Modern observation is not just visual. Thermal sensors see *heat* — a warm body, an
engine, a recently used fire, a vehicle's tire tracks. Practical defensive measures:

- Put terrain mass (earth, walls, dense foliage, foam/space-blanket layers) between
  you and an overhead sensor; cold mass blocks heat.
- Minimize and conceal heat sources (cooking, engines, electronics); use them briefly
  and under cover.
- Understand that standard visual camo does **not** hide a thermal signature — manage
  heat separately.

## Concealment vs. cover

- **Concealment** hides you from view (foliage, smoke, darkness, a bush) but does not
  stop projectiles.
- **Cover** stops fragments/rounds (earth, masonry, vehicles' engine blocks, dirt-filled
  barriers). In the drone era, **overhead** cover matters as much as frontal cover.
- Prefer positions that give both. When digging in, overhead protection against
  top-attack munitions is a recurring lesson from recent conflicts.

## Position discipline

- Site positions in shadow with a backdrop and an escape route.
- Track conditioning: hide tracks, spoil, and disturbed vegetation that reveal a
  position from above.
- Maintain noise and light discipline; a single light or sound at night is a beacon.
- Rotate, disperse, and avoid creating obvious patterns (paths, latrines, antennas).
