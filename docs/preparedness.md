# Emergency preparedness

> **Disclaimer:** Educational / reference / preparedness material with links to public and open-source resources. Not operational instructions for violence or weapons. Verify against primary sources; train with qualified instructors. Follow all applicable laws.

Emergency preparedness for civilians covers the knowledge, supplies, and plans needed to survive and recover from natural or man-made disasters with minimal dependence on outside help for at least 72 hours — and ideally two weeks. The core framework taught by FEMA, Ready.gov, and the American Red Cross rests on three pillars: Build a Kit (stockpile essential supplies), Make a Plan (coordinate family communications and actions), and Be Informed (understand local hazards and alert systems). Preparedness is not about worst-case paranoia; it is practical household resilience. Most major disasters — hurricanes, ice storms, wildfires, earthquakes, extended power outages — cut access to utilities, pharmacies, grocery stores, and first responders for days. Pre-positioned supplies, a written family plan, and awareness of local warning systems can mean the difference between a manageable disruption and a life-threatening crisis. This reference covers kit contents from 72-hour go-bags through two-week home caches, water storage and purification, family communication and evacuation planning, shelter-in-place decisions, power outage safety, special-needs considerations, and training resources open to all civilians.

## The Three Pillars: Build a Kit, Make a Plan, Be Informed

FEMA and Ready.gov organize civilian preparedness around three actions.

1. BUILD A KIT: Assemble supplies before a disaster strikes. The baseline is a 72-hour (3-day) portable kit — the go-bag or bug-out bag — that you can grab when you must leave quickly. Beyond that, maintain a two-week home cache for sheltering in place during extended disruptions such as major winter storms or prolonged power outages. FEMA notes that approximately half of Americans take a prescription medicine daily, making medication continuity a critical planning item alongside food and water.

2. MAKE A PLAN: A written family emergency plan answers four questions before a crisis occurs: (a) How will family members reach each other? (b) Where will we meet locally if we cannot return home? (c) Where will we meet outside our immediate area if we must evacuate further? (d) Who is our designated out-of-area contact? Local calls often fail during disasters while long-distance lines remain open, so choosing an out-of-state contact point is a practical tactic. Ready.gov provides fillable wallet-card and full-page templates at ready.gov/plan.

3. BE INFORMED: Know which hazards are common in your area (flood zones, wildfire risk, earthquake faults, hurricane tracks, tornado corridors), how your community issues warnings, and what the warnings mean. Sign up for local emergency alerts through your county emergency management website. Download the FEMA App (free, iOS and Android) for real-time National Weather Service alerts and disaster preparedness guides.

## Water Storage and Purification

Water is the single most critical supply item. The human body can survive weeks without food but only about three days without water.

STORAGE BASELINE: Store a minimum of 1 gallon per person per day. FEMA recommends at least a 3-day supply for evacuation (i.e., a go-bag), and a 2-week supply at home. A normally active adult needs approximately 3/4 gallon per day for drinking; the remainder covers sanitation. Children, nursing mothers, people who are sick, and anyone doing hard physical work in hot weather need significantly more — up to double in extreme heat.

STORAGE CONTAINERS: Use food-grade water storage containers with tight-fitting caps (commercially available in 5–55 gallon sizes). Commercially bottled water in sealed original containers is acceptable and has a defined expiration date. Clean PET plastic soda bottles (2-liter) are acceptable for short-term storage — sanitize with a solution of 1 teaspoon of unscented household bleach per quart of water before filling. Do not use containers that previously held milk or juice; residual sugars promote bacteria. Store containers in a cool, dark place away from petroleum products (plastic absorbs vapors). Replace stored water every six months unless commercially sealed.

PURIFICATION METHODS (CDC / Ready.gov):
- Boiling: Bring water to a rolling boil for 1 full minute (3 minutes above 6,500 ft elevation). Let cool before drinking. Pour back and forth between two clean containers to improve taste.
- Unscented liquid household bleach (5.25–6.0% sodium hypochlorite): Add 1/8 teaspoon (8 drops) per gallon of clear water, or 1/4 teaspoon per gallon of cloudy water. Stir, let stand 30 minutes. Water should have a faint bleach odor; if not, repeat and wait another 15 minutes. If still no odor, discard and find another source. Do NOT use scented bleach, color-safe bleach, or bleach with added cleaners.
- Commercial water purification tablets (iodine or sodium dichloroisocyanurate): Follow package directions; generally not recommended for pregnant women, people with thyroid conditions, or long-term use.
- Portable water filters rated to remove Giardia and Cryptosporidium (e.g., hollow-fiber or ceramic filters): useful for wilderness or post-disaster field use.

FEMA's full water guidance: ready.gov/water. CDC water emergency guidance: cdc.gov/water-emergency.

## 72-Hour Go-Bag and Extended Home Kit

A go-bag is a portable emergency supply kit kept ready for rapid evacuation. It should be stored near an exit point and known to all household members. Beyond the go-bag, maintain a larger home cache targeting two weeks of supplies.

GO-BAG CORE CONTENTS (per Ready.gov / FEMA):
- Water: 1 gallon per person (minimum; supplement with water purification capability)
- Food: at least 3-day supply of non-perishable, easy-to-prepare items (no cooking required preferred)
- Battery-powered or hand-crank radio (NOAA Weather Radio capable)
- Flashlight with extra batteries (or hand-crank/solar)
- First aid kit (see checklist section)
- Whistle (to signal for help)
- Dust masks or N95 respirators
- Plastic sheeting and duct tape (shelter-in-place use)
- Moist towelettes, garbage bags, plastic ties (personal sanitation)
- Wrench or pliers (to shut off utilities)
- Manual can opener
- Local maps (printed; do not rely solely on phone GPS)
- Cell phone with chargers and a backup battery bank
- Prescription medications (at least a 7-day supply, in original labeled containers)
- Copies of important documents in a waterproof container: ID, insurance policies, bank info, medical records, emergency contact list
- Extra cash in small bills (ATMs and card readers may be offline)
- Sleeping bag or warm blanket per person
- Change of clothing and sturdy shoes per person
- Fire extinguisher (ABC type)
- Matches in a waterproof container
- Feminine hygiene supplies
- Infant formula, diapers, bottles if applicable
- Pet food, extra water, medications, carrier, and vet records if pets are in household

EXTENDED HOME CACHE ADDITIONS (2-week target):
- 14 gallons of water per person (stored in approved containers or commercially bottled)
- 2-week non-perishable food supply (2,000 calories/adult/day): canned goods, dried beans, rice, pasta, freeze-dried meals (25-year shelf life options available), peanut butter, crackers, jerky, trail mix, comfort foods
- Propane or camp stove with extra fuel (for outdoor cooking during power outages)
- Generator with at least 5 gallons of stabilized fuel (store safely, away from living areas)
- Extended first aid supplies
- Extra prescription medications (discuss 90-day fills with provider)
- Sanitation supplies: portable toilet, extra toilet paper, hand sanitizer, bleach
- Entertainment and morale items: books, games, comfort items for children

Kit storage: store in airtight plastic bags inside one or two easy-to-carry containers (plastic bins or duffel bags). Label them clearly. Check and refresh supplies every 6–12 months; rotate food and water on a schedule.

## Family Emergency and Communication Plan

A written plan completed before a disaster is far more reliable than trying to coordinate under stress. Ready.gov provides fillable templates at ready.gov/plan and ready.gov/plan-form, including a wallet-card version.

KEY COMPONENTS:
1. OUT-OF-AREA CONTACT: Designate one person outside your region as the family contact hub. During a local disaster, long-distance calls may connect when local calls cannot. Every household member should have this number memorized and written on a physical card.
2. TWO MEETING POINTS: (a) A nearby meeting spot (e.g., a neighbor's house or a landmark just outside your home) for fires or immediate neighborhood emergencies. (b) A distant meeting spot outside your neighborhood if you cannot return home (e.g., a library, community center, or a relative's home in a neighboring town).
3. COMMUNICATION METHODS: Do not rely on a single method. Cell networks often congest during disasters. Agree on text messaging as a primary backup (texts queue and deliver when capacity returns), then establish backup methods: a landline (non-cordless), a battery/hand-crank radio for receiving alerts, and a pre-agreed check-in schedule.
4. HOUSEHOLD ROSTER: Record names, ages, medications, medical conditions, and special needs for every person and pet in the household. Keep a copy in the kit and digitally.
5. SCHOOL AND WORKPLACE PLANS: Learn your employer's and your children's school emergency plans. Know under what conditions children will be held at school vs. released. Have an authorized pickup list on file.
6. NEIGHBOR NETWORK: FEMA encourages knowing your neighbors, particularly if any are elderly, have disabilities, or live alone. Coordinated neighborhood preparedness increases overall resilience.
7. PETS AND LIVESTOCK: Plan for animal needs explicitly. Most public emergency shelters do not accept pets (only service animals). Identify pet-friendly hotels along your evacuation route, and boarding facilities or animal shelters that accept pets during disasters.

## Shelter-in-Place vs. Evacuation: When to Stay, When to Go

One of the most critical preparedness decisions is whether to shelter in place or evacuate. The wrong choice can be fatal. Always follow instructions from local emergency management officials and law enforcement — they have ground-level situational awareness.

SHELTER-IN-PLACE: Remain inside a structure to gain protection from an external hazard. Best used when:
- Evacuating would expose you to greater risk than remaining (e.g., a fast-moving chemical plume, an active shooter situation, or extremely dangerous road conditions)
- Local authorities direct you to shelter in place
- The event is short-duration or the hazard is localized
- Your home is structurally sound and not in a flood plain or fire path
How to shelter in place for a chemical/biological hazard: go to an interior room above ground level (toxins often hug the ground), shut all windows and doors, turn off HVAC and fans, use plastic sheeting and duct tape to seal gaps if directed, listen to emergency broadcasts, and wait for the all-clear.

EVACUATION: Leave the area to escape a hazard. Evacuation is necessary when:
- Local authorities issue a mandatory evacuation order (you must comply)
- Your home is in a flood zone, storm surge zone, wildfire evacuation zone, or is structurally compromised
- A chemical plume or smoke is expected to linger or local conditions worsen
- You are in a mobile home or manufactured housing when high winds or flooding threaten (mobile homes are not safe in these events; always evacuate to a sturdier building)
EVACUATION TIPS:
- Know multiple routes out of your area and practice them
- Learn your state and county designated evacuation routes (posted by state DOT and emergency management agencies)
- Do not wait for a mandatory order if you have mobility challenges, transportation needs, or pets — leave early
- Bring your go-bag, important documents, medications, and pets
- Tell your out-of-area contact where you are going and your expected route
- Check fuel before an approaching storm; gas stations may run out
- Pre-identify pet-friendly hotels and shelters along your route
- Public emergency shelters are typically managed by the Red Cross or local emergency management; only service animals are allowed in most public shelters

FEMA's full guidance: fema.gov/emergency-managers/national-preparedness/plan/evacuation-shelter-in-place and ready.gov/shelter.

## Power Outage Preparedness and Safety

Extended power outages are among the most common and most disruptive civilian emergencies, triggered by severe weather, wildfires, ice storms, or infrastructure failures. FEMA and the Red Cross treat power outage preparedness as a distinct category.

BEFORE AN OUTAGE:
- Keep a flashlight and batteries in an easily accessible location in every room
- Purchase a battery-powered or hand-crank NOAA Weather Radio
- Maintain a backup battery bank for mobile phones
- Keep non-cordless landline phone (standard corded phones work without household power)
- Stock a cooler and ice packs to extend food refrigeration
- Identify any household members who depend on electrically powered medical equipment (CPAP, home oxygen concentrators, dialysis) and have a backup plan arranged with their medical provider and possibly the local utility company (most utilities maintain a medical baseline registry)
- Fill your car's gas tank before a storm; stations may have no power for pumps

DURING AN OUTAGE:
- Food safety: an unopened refrigerator keeps food safe approximately 4 hours; an unopened full freezer keeps temperature for about 48 hours (24 hours if half full). Use a thermometer. Discard any food that has been above 40°F (4°C) for more than 2 hours.
- Use flashlights, not candles (fire hazard). If candles are necessary, never leave them unattended.
- Unplug major appliances and electronics before power returns to prevent damage from power surges. Leave one lamp plugged in as an indicator that power has returned.
- Turn refrigerator and freezer to coldest settings before a known outage event.

GENERATOR SAFETY (CRITICAL — CARBON MONOXIDE):
- NEVER operate a generator, gas grill, camp stove, or charcoal grill indoors, in a garage, carport, basement, or any enclosed or partially enclosed space — even with doors and windows open.
- Place generators at least 20 feet from windows, doors, and vents.
- Install battery-operated carbon monoxide (CO) detectors on every level of your home and outside sleeping areas. CO is odorless and colorless — it kills without warning.
- Connect appliances directly to the generator using heavy-duty extension cords rated for outdoor use; do not connect a generator to household wiring (backfeed can kill utility workers).
- Let the generator cool before refueling.

MEDICAL DEVICES AND REFRIGERATED MEDICATIONS:
- Talk to your provider in advance about how long medications can be kept at higher temperatures. Many insulin formulations, for example, have specific temperature requirements.
- Contact your power company to register on their medical priority list if a household member requires power-dependent equipment.

Ready.gov power outage hazard information sheet: ready.gov/sites/default/files/2024-03/ready.gov_power-outage_hazard-info-sheet.pdf
Red Cross power outage safety: redcross.org/get-help/how-to-prepare-for-emergencies/types-of-emergencies/power-outage.html

## Special Needs, Vulnerable Populations, and Pets

Standard preparedness advice assumes an able-bodied adult household. FEMA's Ready.gov has dedicated guidance for people with disabilities, older adults, and pet owners.

PEOPLE WITH DISABILITIES AND ACCESS/FUNCTIONAL NEEDS:
- Assess daily resource dependencies: medications, medical equipment, mobility aids (wheelchairs, walkers, canes), communication devices, refrigerated medications, dialysis, home oxygen
- Create a support network of neighbors, family, and friends who know your plan and can assist during an emergency
- Wear medical alert tags or bracelets; store a medical information summary in your kit
- If you use assistive technology, plan how to evacuate with it or how to replace it; have a backup communication plan if your primary AAC device is unavailable
- Keep a list of local disability-specific resources (accessible transportation providers, medical equipment suppliers) in your kit
- Register with your local emergency management office if you may need evacuation assistance
- Ready.gov disability preparedness guide: ready.gov/people-disabilities

OLDER ADULTS:
- FEMA's Disaster Preparedness Guide for Older Adults (2023) provides a three-step framework: assess your needs, create a plan, engage your network
- Heat and cold are especially dangerous for older adults; have a plan for temperature extremes during power outages
- Keep a 7-day supply of all medications readily accessible in the kit
- Ready.gov older adults: ready.gov/older-adults

CHILDREN:
- Teach children how to call 911, what the family meeting points are, and who the out-of-area contact is
- Keep comfort items (stuffed animals, books) in the kit to reduce stress
- Stock infant formula, diapers, bottles, wipes, and any pediatric medications
- Practice emergency drills regularly — fire escape routes, drop-cover-hold-on for earthquakes, etc.

PETS:
- Include pets explicitly in your emergency plan
- Go-bag for pets: 3-day food and water supply, food and water bowls, medications with records, vaccination records, leash/collar with ID tag, carrier or crate, recent photo of you with your pet (proves ownership), waste bags/litter, comfort item
- Identify pet-friendly hotels, boarding facilities, and animal shelters along your evacuation routes in advance
- Most public emergency shelters only permit service animals; do not assume your pet will be admitted
- FEMA pet preparedness: fema.gov/press-release/20250121/include-pets-needs-your-disaster-plan and ready.gov/pets

## Alert Systems, Training Resources, and Financial Preparedness

ALERT AND WARNING SYSTEMS:
The U.S. uses a layered system of public alerts:
- Wireless Emergency Alerts (WEA): Short messages pushed automatically to WEA-enabled cell phones in a targeted geographic area. No opt-in required. Issued for Extreme Threats (tornadoes, flash floods), Imminent Threats (severe thunderstorms), AMBER Alerts, and Presidential alerts.
- Emergency Alert System (EAS): Broadcasts over TV and radio. NOAA Weather Radio All Hazards (NWR) is the backbone — a nationwide network of stations broadcasting continuous weather and hazard information 24/7 directly from the nearest National Weather Service office. Battery-powered or hand-crank NWR receivers are a core kit item.
- Integrated Public Alert and Warning System (IPAWS): FEMA's national platform enabling federal, state, local, tribal, and territorial authorities to send simultaneous WEA, EAS, and NWR alerts.
- Local notification systems: Many counties and cities run supplemental opt-in alert systems (text/email/phone call). Sign up through your county emergency management agency website.
- FEMA App: Free app (iOS and Android) providing real-time NWS alerts for up to five locations, preparedness checklists, shelter-finder, and disaster recovery resources.

FIRST AID AND MEDICAL TRAINING:
- American Red Cross First Aid/CPR/AED courses: classroom and online options at redcross.org/take-a-class
- Stop the Bleed (ACS): nationwide program teaching civilians to control life-threatening bleeding using direct pressure, wound packing, and tourniquets. Free training widely available. stopthebleed.org
- Red Cross 'Until Help Arrives': free online course on life-saving bystander actions (severe bleeding, CPR, choking, shock). redcross.org/take-a-class/first-aid/first-aid-training/first-aid-classes/until-help-arrives
- Red Cross First Aid for Severe Trauma (FAST): Stop the Bleed-aligned course, free for high school students.

COMMUNITY PREPAREDNESS TRAINING:
- Community Emergency Response Team (CERT): FEMA-sponsored program that trains civilians in basic disaster response skills — fire safety, light search and rescue, team organization, disaster medical operations. Free training offered by local fire/police/emergency management agencies. Find a local program at community.fema.gov. Online introduction: FEMA IS-317 at training.fema.gov.
- FEMA Independent Study (IS) courses: free online courses at training.fema.gov covering earthquake safety, flood preparedness, pandemic planning, and dozens of other topics. No registration fee.

FINANCIAL PREPAREDNESS:
- FEMA's Emergency Financial First Aid Kit (EFFAK), developed with Operation HOPE, helps households organize critical financial, medical, and legal documents before a disaster. Available free at ready.gov/emergency-financial-first-aid-kit.
- Documents to safeguard (waterproof container + offsite/digital copy): government-issued IDs (passport, driver's license, birth certificates, Social Security cards), insurance policies (home/renters, auto, life, health), bank account and investment account information, mortgage/lease documents, utility account numbers, medical records and current medication list, will and power of attorney documents, veterinary records for pets.
- Store paper copies in a fireproof/waterproof home safe AND in a bank safety deposit box, with a trusted person outside your area, or in encrypted cloud storage.
- Keep emergency cash on hand in small bills — ATMs and card readers may be offline for days after a major disaster. The recommended minimum is enough to cover several days of food and fuel.

### Checklist: Water Purification Quick Reference

- [ ] BOILING: Bring to a rolling boil for 1 minute (3 minutes above 6,500 ft). Let cool before drinking.
- [ ] BLEACH (clear water): 1/8 teaspoon (8 drops) of 5.25-6% unscented sodium hypochlorite bleach per gallon. Mix, wait 30 minutes. Should smell faintly of chlorine.
- [ ] BLEACH (cloudy water): 1/4 teaspoon (16 drops) per gallon. Filter through clean cloth first.
- [ ] If no chlorine odor after 30 minutes: repeat dose, wait 15 more minutes. If still no odor, discard water.
- [ ] Do NOT use: scented bleach, color-safe bleach, or bleach with added cleaners.
- [ ] Portable ceramic or hollow-fiber filter: effective for Giardia, Cryptosporidium (not viruses alone).
- [ ] Iodine tablets: follow package directions; avoid if pregnant, thyroid condition, or long-term use.
- [ ] Replace stored non-commercial water every 6 months.
- [ ] Store containers away from petroleum products; plastic absorbs vapors.

### Checklist: Family Communication Plan (Key Elements to Complete)

- [ ] Designate one out-of-area contact: name, phone number, email (everyone memorizes this number)
- [ ] Local meeting point #1: just outside home (example: mailbox, neighbor's house)
- [ ] Local meeting point #2: outside neighborhood if you cannot return home (library, school, community center)
- [ ] Out-of-area meeting destination if regional evacuation needed (relative's address, specific hotel chain)
- [ ] Confirm how children will be picked up from school and who is an authorized alternate pickup
- [ ] Know your workplace or school emergency protocols
- [ ] Agree on primary and backup communication methods (text first, then calls, then email)
- [ ] All household members carry physical wallet card with contact info (Ready.gov template: ready.gov/sites/default/files/2025-06/family-communication-plan_fillable-card.pdf)
- [ ] Sign up for local Wireless Emergency Alerts (confirm cell phones are WEA-enabled; default on most US phones)
- [ ] Sign up for county opt-in alert system (text/email) at your county emergency management website
- [ ] Download FEMA App and configure alerts for your location and up to 4 other locations
- [ ] Know your evacuation zone designation (if applicable) and designated state evacuation routes

### Checklist: Power Outage Safety Checklist

- [ ] NEVER use generator, grill, or camp stove indoors or in a garage — minimum 20 feet from windows
- [ ] Install battery-operated CO detectors on every home level and outside sleeping areas
- [ ] Unplug major appliances when power fails (protect against surge when power returns)
- [ ] Leave one lamp plugged in as an indicator that power has returned
- [ ] Keep refrigerator closed: safe approx. 4 hours; freezer safe approx. 48 hours (full, door closed)
- [ ] Discard food above 40°F (4°C) for more than 2 hours
- [ ] Use flashlights instead of candles; if candles necessary, never leave unattended
- [ ] Know how to manually release your garage door opener
- [ ] Check on neighbors — especially elderly, disabled, or those with medical equipment
- [ ] If on power-dependent medical equipment, contact provider and utility company in advance for backup plan
- [ ] Keep vehicle fuel tank at least half-full at all times in storm-prone seasons
