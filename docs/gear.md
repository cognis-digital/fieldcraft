# Gear, kit & EDC

> **Disclaimer:** Educational / reference / preparedness material with links to public and open-source resources. Not operational instructions for violence or weapons. Verify against primary sources; train with qualified instructors. Follow all applicable laws.

This reference covers practical gear selection, kit organization, and everyday carry (EDC) principles for civilians and military personnel. It integrates three canonical frameworks: Dave Canterbury's 10 Cs of Survivability (bushcraft/survival kit logic), The Mountaineers' 10 Essentials (outdoor safety systems), and the U.S. Army ECWCS layering doctrine. Together these frameworks address the core problem of field survivability: maintaining body temperature, staying oriented, treating injury, and sustaining life across a wide range of conditions. Recognized authoritative sources include Ready.gov (FEMA), American Red Cross, Stop the Bleed / ACS, U.S. Army Technical Manuals (TM 10-8415-236-10), Army FM 3-05.70 / FM 21-76, The Mountaineers, REI Expert Advice, and Washington Trails Association.

## The 10 Cs of Survivability (Dave Canterbury Framework)

Dave Canterbury, author of Bushcraft 101, developed a tiered kit-building system where the first 5 Cs are essential for immediate survival, and the second 5 add comfort and resilience. The framework is widely used for building bug-out bags, bushcraft kits, and survival caches.

**First 5 Cs (Critical):**
1. Cutting Tool — A high-quality fixed-blade knife (full-tang, carbon or stainless steel) is the single most important item. Used for processing wood, making fire, food prep, shelter construction, and field repairs. A good fixed blade like a Morakniv Garberg or ESEE-6 (1095 high-carbon steel) is preferred for durability over a folder in true survival contexts.
2. Combustion Device — A means to make fire: BIC lighter, ferro rod (ferrocerium), waterproof matches, or a combination. Fire provides warmth, water purification, cooking, signaling, and morale.
3. Cover — Emergency or field-expedient shelter: wool blanket, tarp, emergency bivy, or a purpose-built shelter. Protects against exposure — the primary killer in wilderness emergencies.
4. Container — A 32 oz stainless steel wide-mouth bottle minimum. Allows boiling of water, thus disinfection by heat. Nesting cup optional. Never rely solely on plastic in a survival context.
5. Cordage — 550 paracord is the standard. 50–100 feet covers most needs: shelter lashing, snares, gear repair, improvised harness, tourniquet improvisation.

**Second 5 Cs (Important):**
6. Cotton Bandana — Multipurpose: sun protection, wound covering (not a sterile dressing), improvised water pre-filter, fire-starting char cloth.
7. Compass — A baseplate compass (Silva, Suunto) remains essential even with GPS devices present. Magnetic compasses work without batteries. Paired with a topo map, they are the fundamental navigation redundancy.
8. Candle (Illumination) — Canterbury used candle; contemporary builds substitute a quality headlamp (hands-free). A separate pocket light or candle is a valid backup.
9. Cloth Sail Needle — Heavy-gauge needle for repairing gear, tarps, clothing. Can serve limited wound-closure roles in extremis (though TCCC-trained personnel carry proper closure supplies).
10. Cargo Tape — Duct tape or Gorilla tape. Gear repair, improvised splinting, blister prevention (applied over sock liner), sealing punctures in rain gear.

Note: Canterbury's framework is skill-centric — these items have maximum value only with practiced bushcraft skills. The items are meant to be carried ON your person, not just in a base camp pack.

## The 10 Essentials — Systems Approach (Mountaineers / REI)

Originally compiled by The Mountaineers (Seattle) in the 1930s as a list of individual items, the Ten Essentials have evolved into a modern systems approach. The systems approach recognizes that what matters is the function, not the specific item — technology changes, but the need to navigate, stay warm, and treat injury does not.

**The 10 Essential Systems:**
1. Navigation — Topographic map of the area (paper, waterproofed), baseplate compass, and ideally a GPS device or satellite communicator (e.g., Garmin inReach Mini). Know how to triangulate, set a bearing, and follow a bearing before heading out. Paper map + compass is the non-electronic redundancy.
2. Sun Protection — SPF 30+ sunscreen (stick versions for pack-and-go), UV-blocking sunglasses, wide-brim hat, UPF-rated clothing. High altitude and snow significantly amplify UV exposure.
3. Insulation — Extra layers beyond the minimum expected: puffy or fleece jacket, rain jacket, warm hat, gloves, extra socks. Hypothermia can occur in summer temperatures with wind and moisture.
4. Illumination — Headlamp preferred (hands-free). Bring spare batteries or a backup light. Modern headlamps: 100–400 lumens with flood/spot modes and red-light mode for night vision preservation. AAA and 18650-based models are common; 18650 gives longer runtime.
5. First-Aid Supplies — Full first-aid kit plus blister treatment (moleskin), SAM splint, elastic bandage, and knowledge of use. For serious backcountry: IFAK components (tourniquet, chest seal, hemostatic gauze). Red Cross and NOLS Wilderness First Aid courses teach field-relevant skills.
6. Fire Starter — Primary lighter (BIC), backup ferro rod + tinder (fatwood shavings, petroleum-impregnated cotton). Fire for signaling (three fires in a triangle = universal distress signal), warmth, and water purification.
7. Repair Kit and Tools — Multi-tool or knife, duct tape, repair patches (tent, rain jacket), safety pins, cable ties, extra cordage. Gear failures in the field are common.
8. Nutrition — Extra food beyond trip duration: high-calorie, non-perishable snacks (energy bars, jerky, nuts, dried fruit). Minimum 200+ extra calories per person per day planned beyond expected need.
9. Hydration — Water filter (Sawyer Squeeze, 0.1 micron, removes bacteria and protozoa), chemical backup (Aquamira drops or Micropur tabs, which kill viruses that filters miss), and a minimum 2-liter carrying capacity. SteriPEN UV purifiers work in clear water and kill viruses.
10. Emergency Shelter — Bivy sack or reflective emergency space blanket as minimum. Ultralight tarp (silnylon, 6–8 oz) extends usability. Prevents hypothermia in an unplanned overnight stay.

The 10 Essentials do not define specific brands — they define functions. Contents are scaled to trip length, terrain, group size, and conditions.

## Layering Systems: Civilian and Military

Layering is the foundational principle of field clothing: separate garments handle separate thermal functions rather than relying on one bulky garment. This allows fine-grained regulation of body temperature during varying exertion and weather conditions.

**Civilian Three-Layer System:**
- Layer 1 — Base Layer (Wicking): Worn directly against skin. Moves (wicks) sweat away from the body to prevent evaporative chilling. Do NOT use cotton — 'Cotton kills' is the field adage because wet cotton has near-zero insulating value. Best materials: merino wool (naturally odor-resistant, warm when wet, comfortable) or synthetic polyester (dries faster, less expensive, more durable under heavy abrasion). Merino is preferred for multi-day trips where you cannot wash; synthetic for high-output day trips.
- Layer 2 — Mid Layer (Insulation): Traps warm air. Options: fleece (Polartec 100, 200, 300 — heavier weight = more warmth), wool sweater, synthetic insulated jacket (Primaloft, Climashield), or down jacket (do not get wet — down loses insulating value when soaked; synthetic keeps insulating when wet). Midweight fleece (200 weight) is the all-around workhorse.
- Layer 3 — Outer Layer (Shell): Blocks wind and precipitation. Hard shell: waterproof/breathable GORE-TEX or equivalent; best for sustained rain. Soft shell: wind-resistant, stretch, highly breathable; best for aerobic activity in cool/dry conditions. A hardshell over a fleece mid-layer handles most temperate-to-alpine conditions.

**U.S. Army ECWCS Gen III (7-Level Military Layering):**
The Extended Cold Weather Clothing System Gen III (governed by TM 10-8415-236-10) operates across a -60°F to +40°F range via 12 components across 7 levels:
- Level 1: Lightweight base (Polartec Power Dry) — wicking, worn next to skin
- Level 2: Midweight base (Polartec) — wicking + light insulation, can be outer layer in mild conditions
- Level 3: Fleece jacket/trousers (Polartec) — primary insulation layer
- Level 4: Wind jacket (nylon) — lightweight wind protection, breathable
- Level 5: Soft shell jacket/trousers — moderate cold/wet, active movement
- Level 6: GORE-TEX hard shell jacket/trousers — waterproof/windproof outer layer
- Level 7: Extreme cold weather parka + trousers (high-loft batting) — extreme cold (colloquially the 'marshmallow suit')

Soldierly principle: 'Dress in layers, avoid cotton, stay dry.' The military doctrine additionally emphasizes the 'COLD' acronym for cold weather safety: Keep it Clean, Avoid Overheating, Wear Loose and in Layers, Keep it Dry.

**Footwear Layering:**
Socks follow the same principle. Liner sock (thin moisture-wicking synthetic or merino) + outer cushion sock (thicker merino or wool blend). The liner sock reduces blister-causing friction by allowing movement between liner and outer sock rather than between sock and skin. Merino performs best for multi-day use (odor, warmth when wet). Synthetic dries faster and resists abrasion better for high-output single-day trips. Double up in cold: liner + thicker wool outer.

## Footwear Selection: Boots, Trail Runners, and Socks

Footwear is the most critical gear decision for any foot-mobile operation — blisters, sprained ankles, and trench foot have ended more field efforts than equipment failure. The selection depends on terrain, load, duration, and weather.

**Hiking Boots:**
- High ankle collar provides the best lateral stability and torsional rigidity
- Suited for: heavy loads (>30 lbs), rough/rocky/technical terrain, wet or cold conditions, multi-day travel
- Trade-offs: heavier (1.5–3 lbs/pair), require break-in period, generate more heat (potential blister risk in warm conditions)
- Waterproof boots (GORE-TEX lined): keep feet dry in wet conditions but trap moisture when sweating — trade-off between external wet and internal wet
- Military: Berry-compliant tactical boots (Bates, Belleville, Danner) add ballistic/puncture protection; desert vs. jungle vs. temperate models vary significantly

**Trail Running Shoes:**
- Light, flexible, fast-drying, immediate comfort with no break-in
- Preferred by: thru-hikers, ultralight backpackers, multi-day trail runners
- Trade-offs: less lateral support, less durable on sustained rocky terrain under heavy loads, less protection in cold/wet
- Evidence strongly favors trail runners for blister reduction — the narrower contact between shoe and foot and the flexibility reduce hot spots
- Pair with thin, moisture-wicking socks (not thick hiking socks designed for stiff boots)

**Sock System:**
- Merino wool: warm when wet, odor-resistant over multiple days, slightly less durable than synthetic
- Synthetic (polyester/nylon blend): faster-drying, more durable, less expensive
- Best practice for multi-day: merino wool mid-weight, liner sock optional
- Critical: Do NOT wear cotton socks in the field
- Break-in new footwear with actual socks before field use; test on terrain similar to planned use
- Carry at least one dry spare pair sealed in a waterproof bag

## EDC (Everyday Carry): Core Components

EDC refers to items carried on the person daily or maintained in a immediately-accessible state. A sound EDC kit balances utility, portability, and legal compliance with local laws (knife laws vary significantly by jurisdiction).

**Tier 1 — On-person carry (always):**
- Pocket knife / folding blade: A quality folding knife (Benchmade, Spyderco, CRKT) handles 95% of civilian cutting tasks. Look for a liner or frame lock, steel with good edge retention (S30V, 154CM, AUS-8), and a pocket clip. Blade length 2.5–3.5 inches suits most urban/suburban legal requirements.
- Flashlight: A dedicated pocket or keychain light is brighter and more reliable than a phone light, and preserves phone battery. EDC range: 100–600 lumens. AA-battery lights (Streamlight ProTac 1AA, 350 lumens) offer standard-cell convenience. 18650 lights offer superior runtime and brightness. Low mode (5–50 lumens) for navigation; full mode for illuminating scenes.
- Pen: Tactical ink pen (Fisher Space Pen, all-weather; or Rite in the Rain pen) writes in moisture, cold, upside-down. Doubles as a self-defense tool and documentation instrument.
- Phone: Navigation (offline maps with Gaia GPS or CalTopo downloaded), communication, emergency alerting, flashlight backup, first aid reference.
- Wallet: Minimize. Cash (small bills), photo ID, emergency contact card, medical info card.
- Watch: A mechanical or G-Shock quartz watch gives time and often compass/barometer/altimeter functions without phone dependency.

**Tier 2 — Day pack / EDC bag:**
- Multi-tool: Leatherman Wave+, Surge, or Charge TI cover pliers, wire cutter, multiple blades, files, screwdrivers, can opener. Multi-tools fill the gap between a pocket knife and a full tool roll.
- Tourniquet (CAT or SOFTT-W): The single highest-impact trauma intervention a layperson can carry. A limb tourniquet correctly applied within 2–3 minutes of arterial injury prevents death. Stop the Bleed training (free, ACS) teaches correct application.
- Headlamp: In the bag/pack for hands-free tasks when the pocket light is insufficient.
- Compact first aid: Adhesive bandages, nitrile gloves, alcohol preps, trauma dressing, SAM splint.
- Water: 1 liter minimum; Nalgene or hydration bladder.
- Snacks: 400+ calories non-perishable.

**Knife Selection — Fixed vs. Folding:**
- Fixed blade: superior strength (no pivot point), easier to clean, better for hard use (batoning, food prep, field tasks). Carried in a sheath on belt or pack. Better choice for wilderness/field environments.
- Folding: concealable, legal in more jurisdictions, daily-carry practical. Adequate for most civilian tasks, rescue, and light field work.
- Quality indicators: full-tang construction (fixed), positive lockup (folder), high-carbon or stainless steel with known metallurgy (avoid mystery steel on cheap knives).

## Kit Organization: MOLLE, Packing Principles, and Load Distribution

How gear is organized and packed is as important as what gear is selected — a disorganized kit costs time and energy when seconds matter.

**MOLLE System:**
MOLLE (Modular Lightweight Load-carrying Equipment) is the current U.S. Army standard load-carrying architecture. MOLLE uses PALS (Pouch Attachment Ladder System) webbing — rows of heavy-duty nylon stitched onto bags, vests, and carriers — allowing modular attachment of pouches and accessories. Key components:
- FLC (Fighting Load Carrier): chest/torso rig holding essential mission items (magazines, IFAK, water)
- Assault pack / patrol pack: 24–72 hr medium pack
- Main rucksack (MOLLE II): large-frame sustained operations pack with sustainment pouches, external frame, and waist belt
- Mission-specific sets: Rifleman, Medic, Grenadier, SAW Gunner

PALS attachment weave: route the accessory's straps through the PALS rows in alternating over/under passes for a secure, non-slip attachment. Civilian MOLLE products follow the same attachment standard.

**Packing Principles:**
1. Heavy items (food, water, shelter) go closest to the back and between shoulder blades — transfers load to the skeletal frame and keeps center of mass close to body.
2. Frequently accessed items (snacks, map, first aid) in hip belt pockets or top lid.
3. Waterproof critical items: electronics, extra clothes, fire-starting material, documents — sealed in dry bags or zip-locks inside the main pack.
4. Bag-in-a-bag method: group similar items (medical, navigation, fire, repair) in labeled stuff sacks or pouches before loading the main pack.
5. Ranger Roll technique: clothing rolled tight (not folded) compresses volume and reduces wrinkle; useful for packing uniform sets into a rucksack.
6. Weight management: for hiking, total pack weight should not exceed 25–30% of body weight. Every ounce carried costs energy over distance.

**The 3-Tier Carry System (Military/Tactical):**
- Tier 1 (Pocket / On-person): items that stay with you if everything else is lost — knife, compass, lighter, water, tourniquet
- Tier 2 (Fighting load / chest rig): mission-critical items accessible in seconds
- Tier 3 (Rucksack / sustainment): shelter, rations, additional clothing, sustainment gear

If forced to abandon a rucksack, Tier 1 keeps you alive. This principle applies equally to civilian emergency preparedness.

## Illumination: Headlamps, Tactical Lights, and Navigation Lighting

Light discipline and source selection affect both capability and battery logistics. Understanding lumen output, runtime, and battery ecosystem allows informed choices.

**Output Tiers:**
- 1–50 lumens: map reading, night vision preservation, close quarters without blinding team members; red mode on most modern headlamps preserves dark adaptation
- 100–400 lumens: trail navigation, camp tasks, searching; the typical EDC and headlamp range
- 500–1,000+ lumens: search and rescue, scene illumination, tactical/law enforcement use; 18650-powered lights

**Battery Ecosystems:**
- AAA: most available worldwide, lightest option; entry-level EDC lights
- AA: stronger capacity than AAA, still widely available; many reliable duty lights (Streamlight ProTac 1AA)
- CR123A: high discharge for tactical lights; Surefire ecosystem; not rechargeable standard versions, but 18650 adapters exist
- 18650 (Li-ion rechargeable): highest runtime and output; best long-term cost; requires charging infrastructure (not available in extended field ops without solar/dynamo)

**Headlamp Principles:**
- Headlamp > handheld for field use — keeps hands free for navigation, climbing, first aid
- Look for: IPX4+ water resistance, lockout mode (prevents accidental battery drain in pack), flood and spot beam options, red/green LEDs for light discipline
- Spare batteries in a sealed bag are non-negotiable for overnight and multi-day operations
- Representative models: Black Diamond Spot 400 (400 lm, AAA), Petzl Actik Core (600 lm, rechargeable), Princeton Tec Remix (300 lm, AAA)

**Tactical Lights:**
- Weapon-mounted lights are outside the scope of this civilian reference; for tactical carry, handheld lights at 500+ lumens with strobe function are used by law enforcement and military for disorientation and identification
- For EDC, a 1-AA or 1-AAA light at 150–300 lumens covers most needs with common-battery convenience

## Trauma and First Aid: IFAK, Stop the Bleed, and Wilderness Medical

Field trauma care has two distinct domains: immediate hemorrhage control (TCCC-derived, military and civilian) and comprehensive wilderness/remote first aid for sustained care without advanced medical resources.

**Hemorrhage Control — Stop the Bleed / TCCC:**
Massive hemorrhage is the leading preventable cause of death from traumatic injury in both military and civilian contexts. The Stop the Bleed campaign (ACS, 2015) distilled TCCC (Tactical Combat Casualty Care) hemorrhage control doctrine into a three-step public training program:
1. Apply direct pressure (hands/wound packing with gauze)
2. Pack the wound (deep wounds — push gauze in firmly)
3. Apply a tourniquet (limb wounds with arterial bleeding)

Tourniquet application: approved devices — CAT (Combat Application Tourniquet) and SOFTT-W (Special Operations Forces Tactical Tourniquet Wide) are the two most evidence-supported designs. Apply 2–3 inches proximal to the wound, tighten until bleeding stops, note time of application. Do NOT remove in the field. The MARCH algorithm (Military) orders priorities: Massive Hemorrhage, Airway, Respirations, Circulation, Hypothermia.

**IFAK (Individual First Aid Kit) Contents — Baseline:**
- CAT or SOFTT-W tourniquet (x2 recommended)
- Hemostatic gauze (QuikClot Combat Gauze or Celox)
- Chest seal (vented, x2 — Hyfin, Russell)
- Compression bandage (Israeli Battle Dressing or similar)
- Nasopharyngeal airway (NPA) 28 Fr with lube
- Nitrile gloves (x2 pairs minimum)
- Permanent marker (for noting tourniquet time)
- Trauma shears

**Wilderness First Aid / WFA / WFR:**
For backcountry contexts without rapid EMS access, higher-level training is essential:
- WFA (Wilderness First Aid): 16–20 hour course, Red Cross, NOLS, SOLO; covers patient assessment, fractures, hypothermia, heat emergencies, anaphylaxis
- WAFA (Wilderness Advanced First Aid): 40+ hour course; deeper coverage
- WFR (Wilderness First Responder): 70–80 hours; standard for trip leaders, guides, SAR personnel; EMT-level assessment with wilderness adaptations

**Civilian Kit Additions Beyond IFAK:**
- SAM splint + elastic bandage (fracture/sprain)
- Triangle bandage and safety pins
- Blister kit: moleskin, Second Skin, Leukotape
- Eye wash/saline
- OTC medications: diphenhydramine (allergic reaction), ibuprofen, antidiarrheal, oral rehydration salts
- EpiPen if prescribed (anaphylaxis risk)
- Prescription medications (30-day supply in go-bag)

Always carry a current first aid manual or laminated reference card. The Red Cross Wilderness & Remote First Aid pocket guide is a compact field reference.

### Checklist: Footwear Selection Decision Matrix

- [ ] Heavy load (>30 lbs) OR technical terrain: use hiking boots with high ankle collar
- [ ] Moderate load, moderate terrain: mid-cut hiking shoes (compromise)
- [ ] Light load, established trails, thru-hike: trail running shoes
- [ ] Cold/wet sustained conditions: waterproof boots (GORE-TEX lined) + moisture-wicking socks
- [ ] Always: avoid cotton socks — use merino wool or synthetic
- [ ] Multi-day trips: bring 2 pairs of socks, dry one while wearing the other
- [ ] Blister prevention: liner sock under outer sock, Leukotape on known hot spots before they blister
- [ ] Break in new footwear with planned socks on similar terrain before field use

### Checklist: ECWCS Gen III Layer Selection Guide

- [ ] Level 1 (Polartec Power Dry): wicking base, mild-cold weather or high exertion
- [ ] Level 2 (Midweight base): cold weather base, adds insulation
- [ ] Level 3 (Fleece jacket/trousers): primary cold-weather insulation layer
- [ ] Level 4 (Wind jacket): add when wind is present, breathable
- [ ] Level 5 (Soft shell): active movement in moderate/cold/dry conditions
- [ ] Level 6 (GORE-TEX hard shell): rain/wet/wind outer protection
- [ ] Level 7 (Extreme cold parka): static or extreme cold (-20°F and below)
- [ ] COLD mnemonic: Keep it Clean, Avoid Overheating, Loose and in Layers, Keep it Dry
