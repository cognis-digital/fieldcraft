# Field & tactical medicine (educational)

> **Disclaimer:** Educational / reference / preparedness material with links to public and open-source resources. Not operational instructions for violence or weapons. Verify against primary sources; train with qualified instructors. Follow all applicable laws.

Tactical Combat Casualty Care (TCCC) and its civilian counterpart Tactical Emergency Casualty Care (TECC) represent the gold standard in evidence-based prehospital trauma management. Originally developed by the US military and refined through two decades of combat experience, these frameworks have been adapted for public education through programs like Stop the Bleed, NAEMT courses, and wilderness medicine curricula. The core principle is the same across all settings: the leading causes of preventable death in trauma — uncontrolled hemorrhage, airway obstruction, and tension pneumothorax — can be managed by trained bystanders and first responders using simple tools and structured protocols. TCCC is organized around three phases (Care Under Fire, Tactical Field Care, and Tactical Evacuation Care) and the MARCH mnemonic. The civilian public can access free or low-cost training through Stop the Bleed, Red Cross, and NAEMT, and free reference manuals through the US Army, Joint Trauma System, and Internet Archive.

## The Three TCCC Phases

TCCC divides care into three phases based on the threat environment:

1. CARE UNDER FIRE (CUF): Care provided while actively under fire. The only medical action typically possible is applying a tourniquet to a life-threatening extremity wound. The priority is suppressing the threat and moving the casualty out of the kill zone. Self-aid or buddy-aid is performed with minimal exposure. 'Return fire and take cover' comes before medical care.

2. TACTICAL FIELD CARE (TFC): Care provided once the immediate threat has been suppressed. This is where the full MARCH protocol is executed. Providers have more time and can perform wound packing, airway management, chest decompression, IV/IO access, and pain/antibiotic administration. Casualty documentation begins here.

3. TACTICAL EVACUATION CARE (TACEVAC): Care provided during movement to a higher level of care, whether by ground vehicle or aircraft. Continuous reassessment, fluid resuscitation, and monitoring for deterioration are priorities. MARCH PAWS expands the standard MARCH mnemonic to include Pain, Antibiotics, Wounds, and Splinting — addressing the extended care needs of evacuation.

For prolonged scenarios where evacuation is delayed hours to days, the PROLONGED FIELD CARE (PFC) framework applies. PFC adds priorities such as whole-blood resuscitation, airway/vent management, telemedicine consultation, and sepsis prevention.

## The MARCH Protocol (Primary Survey)

MARCH is the priority-ordered treatment sequence used in TCCC. It mirrors ABCDE in civilian ATLS but reorders priorities based on combat mortality data:

M — MASSIVE HEMORRHAGE: Addressed first because uncontrolled external bleeding is the #1 cause of preventable combat death. Apply tourniquet for extremity wounds; pack junctional/truncal wounds with hemostatic gauze (QuikClot Combat Gauze or Celox Gauze) and apply direct pressure for a minimum of 3 minutes. A tourniquet should be placed 2-3 inches proximal to the wound and tightened until bright-red bleeding stops and distal pulse is eliminated. Note the time of application.

A — AIRWAY: Assess and open the airway. Unconscious casualties should be placed in the recovery position. A Nasopharyngeal Airway (NPA) — a soft rubber tube inserted through the nostril — can maintain the airway in an unconscious but breathing casualty. Contraindicated with facial/skull trauma. Surgical cricothyroidotomy ('cric') is the definitive field airway when NPA fails.

R — RESPIRATION/BREATHING: Assess for tension pneumothorax (absent breath sounds, tracheal deviation, respiratory distress, worsening shock). Treat all open chest wounds immediately with a vented chest seal. If tension pneumothorax is suspected, perform needle decompression: a 14-gauge 3.25-inch needle/catheter at the 2nd intercostal space mid-clavicular line or 4th-5th ICS anterior axillary line.

C — CIRCULATION/SHOCK: Assess for shock (altered mental status, weak/absent radial pulse). Establish IV or IO access. Administer 1 liter Hextend (or equivalent) if available; modern protocol favors whole blood or 1:1:1 blood products (plasma:platelets:RBCs) over crystalloids. Prevent further heat/fluid loss.

H — HYPOTHERMIA/HEAD: Prevent hypothermia immediately — it severely impairs coagulation, worsening hemorrhage. Remove wet clothing, place insulation UNDER the casualty (ground contact is the greatest heat-loss vector), wrap with emergency space blanket (reflective side in), cover the head. Assess for traumatic brain injury (TBI): check GCS, pupils, and motor function. Prevent secondary brain injury by maintaining oxygenation and blood pressure.

Expanded as MARCH PAWS for tactical evacuation: P=Pain management, A=Antibiotics (combat wound prophylaxis), W=Wound reassessment, S=Splinting of fractures.

## Hemorrhage Control: Tourniquets, Wound Packing, and Pressure

Hemorrhage is responsible for approximately 90% of potentially survivable combat deaths. The three primary techniques are:

TOURNIQUETS: The CoTCCC-recommended devices are the Combat Application Tourniquet Generation 7 (CAT Gen 7) and the SOF Tactical Tourniquet Wide (SOF-T Wide). The CAT Gen 7 is the most widely trained globally and achieves arterial occlusion through a windlass-rod mechanism. Key application steps: (1) place 2-3 inches above the wound; (2) pull the strap extremely tight; (3) twist the windlass rod until bright-red bleeding stops; (4) lock the rod in the clip; (5) write the time of application (T-time) on the strap or the casualty's forehead. Tourniquets are NOT released in the field — that is a hospital decision. 'High and tight' application (as high on the limb as possible) is acceptable in Care Under Fire when exact wound location is unknown.

WOUND PACKING (for junctional/truncal wounds not amenable to tourniquet): Pack hemostatic gauze (z-folded, kaolin-impregnated QuikClot Combat Gauze or chitosan-based Celox Gauze) directly into the wound, pressing down to the deepest point of the bleeding source. Apply firm direct pressure for at least 3 minutes. For deep, narrow-tract junctional wounds (groin, axilla, neck), XStat — a syringe that injects compressed foam sponges which expand to fill the cavity — can achieve hemorrhage control when conventional packing fails.

DIRECT PRESSURE: For wounds not requiring hemostatic agents, sustained firm direct pressure with a gloved hand or Israeli bandage (Emergency Trauma Dressing) for a minimum of 3-5 minutes is effective. The Israeli bandage features a built-in pressure bar to maintain compression hands-free.

JUNCTIONAL HEMORRHAGE (groin, axilla, neck) cannot be controlled by limb tourniquet. Options include wound packing with hemostatic gauze, junctional tourniquets (SAM-JT, JETT), and XStat. These injuries carry very high mortality and require rapid evacuation.

## IFAK Contents: Individual First Aid Kit

The IFAK (Individual First Aid Kit) is a personal trauma kit carried on the body. The military concept — one IFAK per soldier, used only for that soldier by a buddy — ensures that every casualty has immediate access to the supplies needed to address the primary killers. Modern IFAK doctrine is built around TCCC priorities.

CORE TCCC IFAK CONTENTS:
- Tourniquet x1 (CAT Gen 7 or SOF-T Wide) — worn on the outside of the kit for one-handed access
- Hemostatic gauze x1-2 (QuikClot Combat Gauze or Celox Gauze, z-folded)
- Elastic/pressure bandage x1 (Israeli bandage / ETD, 4-6 inch)
- Chest seals x2 — VENTED (Hyfin, HALO, or equivalent) — always buy in pairs for entry and exit wounds
- Nasopharyngeal airway (NPA) 28Fr with lubrication
- Nitrile gloves x2 pairs
- Trauma shears
- Permanent marker (for writing T-time)
- Emergency mylar/space blanket (hypothermia prevention)
- Casualty Card (DD Form 1380 or equivalent)

COMMON CIVILIAN ADDITIONS:
- SAM splint
- Wound closure strips / stapler
- Penlight or small flashlight
- CPR face shield
- Personal medications (EpiPen, prescription items)

IFAK vs AFAK vs MFAK: IFAK is a personal kit for self/buddy care. AFAK (Advanced) adds IV supplies, airway adjuncts, and medications. MFAK (Medic/Mass Casualty) is a larger team resource. US Army IFAK II (fielded 2014) is the current standard military configuration.

Critical storage rule: the tourniquet should always be accessible with one hand, on the outside of the kit or on the body (e.g., chest rig, thigh rig). An IFAK that requires two hands to open has failed its purpose.

## Airway, Breathing, and Chest Trauma

AIRWAY MANAGEMENT HIERARCHY in TCCC:
1. Position — recovery position for unconscious, breathing casualty (prevents aspiration of blood/vomit)
2. Manual maneuvers — chin lift, jaw thrust
3. NPA (Nasopharyngeal Airway) — preferred over OPA in trauma; tolerated in semi-conscious patients; size 28Fr is most common adult size; lubricate, insert through right nostril bevel toward septum, advance along the floor of the nasal cavity. CONTRAINDICATED in suspected skull base fracture (raccoon eyes, Battle's sign, CSF rhinorrhea)
4. Supraglottic airway (SGA) — King LT, i-gel for unconscious apneic patients
5. Surgical cricothyroidotomy — the definitive field airway when all else fails; indicated for airway obstruction that cannot be managed otherwise

CHEST TRAUMA — TWO CRITICAL CONDITIONS:

1. OPEN PNEUMOTHORAX (sucking chest wound): Air enters the pleural space through the wound, collapsing the lung. Treat immediately with a VENTED chest seal over the wound. Vented seals allow air to escape on exhalation (preventing tension), unlike non-vented occlusive dressings which can convert an open to a tension pneumothorax. Apply to a dry, decontaminated surface. If a vented seal is unavailable, a non-vented seal may be used with a finger break on exhalation.

2. TENSION PNEUMOTHORAX: Air accumulates under pressure in the pleural space, collapsing the lung and shifting the mediastinum, compressing the heart and great vessels. RAPIDLY FATAL without treatment. Signs: absent/decreased breath sounds on affected side, respiratory distress, worsening shock, tracheal deviation (late sign), distended neck veins (often absent in hemorrhagic shock). Treat with NEEDLE DECOMPRESSION: 14-gauge 3.25-inch needle-catheter inserted at the 2nd intercostal space mid-clavicular line (ICS2-MCL) or the 4th-5th ICS anterior axillary line (preferred by many because of chest wall thickness). A hiss of escaping air confirms diagnosis. The catheter may kink or clot and require replacement. A finger thoracostomy is the definitive field decompression.

HEMOTHORACX (blood in pleural space): Diagnosed with ultrasound (FAST exam) or clinically. Requires evacuation and surgical drainage (chest tube). Field management: treat associated hemorrhage, maintain oxygenation, evacuate.

## Hypothermia, Heat Injury, and Environmental Emergencies

HYPOTHERMIA IN TRAUMA — THE LETHAL TRIAD:
Hypothermia, acidosis, and coagulopathy form a mutually reinforcing 'lethal triad' in trauma patients. Core temperature below 35C (95F) impairs clotting factor function, worsening hemorrhage. Prevention begins at first contact.

Field Prevention and Treatment:
- Remove wet clothing if operationally feasible (wet fabric conducts heat 25x faster than dry)
- Insulate BENEATH the casualty — ground conduction is the dominant heat-loss route in the field
- Wrap with a space/mylar blanket, reflective surface facing in, and cover the head (up to 40-50% of heat loss occurs through an uncovered head)
- Active warming: chemical heat packs placed in axillae and groin (high-vascularity zones); never place directly on skin
- Hypothermia wrap products (Ready Heat, HPMK) combine vapor barrier + insulation + optional chemical heating
- Warm IV fluids if available in Prolonged Field Care

HEAT EXHAUSTION vs. HEAT STROKE — CRITICAL DISTINCTION:

Heat Exhaustion: Heavy sweating, weakness, pale/clammy skin, fast/weak pulse, nausea, headache, dizziness, near-normal mental status. Core temp typically below 104F (40C). Treatment: move to shade/cool environment, remove excess clothing, oral rehydration if alert, cool with wet cloths and fanning, monitor for progression.

Heat Stroke (EMERGENCY): Hot, dry or minimally sweaty skin, altered mental status (confusion, combativeness, seizure, unconsciousness), core temp above 104F (40C). Mental status change + high temperature = heat stroke until proven otherwise. Treatment is immediate aggressive cooling — cold-water immersion is the most effective method; if unavailable, use ice packs to groin/axillae/neck, wet the skin and fan aggressively (evaporative cooling). Do NOT give aspirin or NSAIDs. Evacuate emergently. Goal: reduce core temp to below 102F (38.9C) as quickly as possible.

DEHYDRATION AND EXERTIONAL HYPONATREMIA: Overhydration with plain water during sustained exertion can cause dangerously low sodium (hyponatremia), mimicking heat exhaustion. Electrolyte replacement is as important as fluid volume in sustained operations.

## TECC: Adapting TCCC for Civilian and Law Enforcement Settings

The Committee for Tactical Emergency Casualty Care (C-TECC) was formed in 2011 to translate military TCCC guidelines for civilian first responders — law enforcement, firefighters, EMS providers, and trained bystanders — responding to mass casualty events, active shooter incidents, and other high-threat civilian emergencies.

KEY DIFFERENCES FROM MILITARY TCCC:
- Civilian providers operate under state/local scope of practice and face civil liability the military does not
- Definitive care (Level 1 trauma centers) is usually minutes away in urban settings vs. hours in combat
- Pediatric patients are a significant consideration in civilian mass casualty events
- Law enforcement first responders have limited medical training and equipment

TECC PHASES (parallel to TCCC):
1. DIRECT THREAT CARE (analogous to Care Under Fire): Bleeding control only; tourniquet application; move out of the threat zone
2. INDIRECT THREAT CARE (analogous to Tactical Field Care): Hemorrhage control, airway, breathing, circulation — full MARCH sequence; dragging/carrying techniques for extracting the wounded
3. EVACUATION CARE (analogous to TACEVAC): Handoff to EMS, continued monitoring

TECC covers: hemorrhage control, surgical airway and needle decompression, pediatric trauma, extraction techniques, and mass casualty triage. The 16-hour NAEMT TECC course is the standard civilian tactical medicine curriculum. C-TECC guidelines are publicly available as free PDFs at c-tecc.org.

HARTFORD CONSENSUS: The Hartford Consensus (THREAT mnemonic) is a related framework developed after Sandy Hook to guide law enforcement-medical integration at active shooter events. THREAT = Threat suppression, Hemorrhage control, Rapid Extrication, Assessment by medical providers, Transport. Stop the Bleed grew directly from Hartford Consensus recommendations.

## Wilderness First Aid and Remote/Prolonged Care

Wilderness First Aid (WFA) and its higher-level certifications (Wilderness First Responder/WFR, Wilderness Emergency Medical Technician/WEMT) address a critical gap: when professional EMS is hours or days away, bystanders and outdoor leaders must manage patients far longer than standard first aid training anticipates.

WFA CORE PRINCIPLES (distinct from urban first aid):
- Patient assessment systems adapted for resource-poor, austere environments
- Decision-making frameworks for when to evacuate vs. treat-and-wait
- Managing patients for 4+ hours without EMS backup
- Understanding improvised equipment and field-expedient techniques
- Environmental injury recognition and treatment (hypothermia, hyperthermia, lightning, altitude illness, envenomation)

PATIENT ASSESSMENT IN THE FIELD (SAMPLE + ABC):
- Scene safety assessment first
- Primary survey: Life threats (major bleeding, airway, breathing, circulation)
- Secondary survey: Head-to-toe physical exam, vital signs, SAMPLE history (Symptoms, Allergies, Medications, Past history, Last intake, Events leading to injury)
- Reassessment every 15 minutes for stable patients, every 5 for unstable

KEY WILDERNESS CONDITIONS:
- Hypothermia management (field rewarming vs. evacuation decision)
- Spinal precautions — WFA applies evidence-based selective immobilization (not blanket C-collar for every MOI)
- Anaphylaxis (epinephrine auto-injector)
- Altitude illness: AMS, HACE, HAPE — descent is the primary treatment
- Snake/insect envenomation: immobilize, keep below heart level, evacuate
- Improvised splinting with SAM splints, trekking poles, sleeping pads
- Burns: cool (not ice), cover, prevent infection, hydrate

TRAINING LADDER:
- Stop the Bleed (2-3 hours, civilian public) — hemorrhage control only
- Red Cross First Aid/CPR/AED (4-8 hours) — foundational
- WFA (16-20 hours, 2 days) — entry-level wilderness
- WFR (70-80 hours, 8-10 days, NOLS/SOLO/WEMS) — professional standard for guides and expedition leaders
- TCCC/TECC (7-16 hours) — tactical/military emphasis
- PHTLS (8-16 hours) — EMS professional trauma focus

### Checklist: MARCH Primary Survey (Tactical Field Care)

- [ ] M — Check for life-threatening extremity bleeding: apply tourniquet 2-3 inches above wound, tighten until bleeding stops, note T-time
- [ ] M — Check for junctional/truncal bleeding: pack wound with hemostatic gauze, apply firm pressure for 3+ minutes
- [ ] A — Assess airway: if unconscious and breathing, place in recovery position
- [ ] A — If airway compromised: insert NPA (28Fr) lubricated, right nostril, directed along floor of nasal cavity
- [ ] A — If NPA fails and airway obstructed: prepare for surgical cricothyroidotomy
- [ ] R — Assess breathing: look/listen/feel for chest rise
- [ ] R — Identify open chest wound: seal immediately with vented chest seal (both entry and exit wounds)
- [ ] R — Assess for tension pneumothorax: absent breath sounds + worsening shock = needle decompression at ICS2-MCL
- [ ] C — Assess for shock: altered mental status, weak/absent radial pulse
- [ ] C — Establish IV/IO access if trained; administer fluids per protocol
- [ ] C — Reassess all hemorrhage controls: tourniquet still effective? Pressure dressing soaked through?
- [ ] H — Remove wet clothing, place insulation under and over casualty
- [ ] H — Apply space blanket reflective-side-in, cover head
- [ ] H — Place chemical heat packs in axillae and groin if available
- [ ] H — Assess for TBI: check GCS, pupils equal and reactive, motor function in all four limbs
- [ ] H — If TBI suspected: prevent hypoxia and hypotension; avoid hyperventilation

### Checklist: Tourniquet Application Checklist

- [ ] Expose the wound to identify bleeding site
- [ ] Apply tourniquet 2-3 inches proximal to the wound (NOT over a joint)
- [ ] Route the strap through the friction buckle and pull extremely tight — tighter than feels right
- [ ] Engage the windlass rod and twist until bright-red bleeding stops AND distal pulse is eliminated
- [ ] Lock the windlass rod in the clip/holder
- [ ] Write the time of application (T-time) on the white label or on the casualty's forehead with marker
- [ ] Do NOT cover the tourniquet — it must be visible for medical handoff
- [ ] Do NOT remove or loosen in the field — hospital decision only
- [ ] If bleeding continues: apply a second tourniquet proximal to the first; do not simply retighten the first

### Checklist: Wound Packing with Hemostatic Gauze

- [ ] Don nitrile gloves
- [ ] Apply direct pressure briefly to assess bleeding and locate the source
- [ ] Open hemostatic gauze (QuikClot Combat Gauze or Celox); do not contaminate
- [ ] Pack gauze DIRECTLY into the wound cavity, pressing down to the deepest point of bleeding
- [ ] Continue packing until the cavity is filled — do not leave air pockets
- [ ] Apply firm, sustained direct pressure with both hands for a minimum of 3 minutes (do not release and check)
- [ ] Apply pressure bandage (Israeli bandage) over the packed wound to maintain compression
- [ ] Reassess: if bleeding has not stopped, repack with additional gauze
- [ ] Mark casualty card or forehead with time and type of hemostatic agent used

### Checklist: Standard IFAK Packing Checklist

- [ ] Tourniquet x1 (CAT Gen 7 or SOF-T Wide) — on exterior of kit or on body for one-hand access
- [ ] Hemostatic gauze x1-2 (QuikClot Combat Gauze or Celox Gauze)
- [ ] Israeli bandage / Emergency Trauma Dressing x1 (4-6 inch)
- [ ] Vented chest seals x2 (Hyfin, HALO, or SAM chest seal) — paired for entry and exit wounds
- [ ] Nasopharyngeal airway 28Fr with lubricant sachet
- [ ] Nitrile gloves x2 pairs
- [ ] Trauma shears
- [ ] Permanent marker (for T-time documentation)
- [ ] Emergency mylar/space blanket
- [ ] Casualty Card (DD Form 1380 or commercial equivalent)
- [ ] Optional: SAM splint, wound closure strips, penlight, personal medications

### Checklist: Heat Stroke Emergency Response

- [ ] Recognize: hot skin (may or may not be sweating) + altered mental status + temperature above 104F (40C)
- [ ] Call 911 / activate EMS immediately
- [ ] Move casualty to shade or cool environment
- [ ] Remove excess clothing and equipment
- [ ] COOL AGGRESSIVELY: cold-water immersion is most effective; if unavailable, soak skin with water and fan vigorously
- [ ] Apply ice packs to neck, axillae (armpits), and groin — high blood-flow areas
- [ ] Do NOT give aspirin, acetaminophen, or NSAIDs — they are not effective for heat stroke and may worsen liver/kidney injury
- [ ] If alert and not vomiting: small sips of cool water or electrolyte solution
- [ ] Continue cooling until core temperature reaches 102F (38.9C) or mental status improves
- [ ] Monitor airway — seizures and vomiting risk
- [ ] Do NOT delay transport — evacuate emergently to definitive care

### Checklist: Hypothermia Prevention in Trauma (Field)

- [ ] Begin hypothermia prevention immediately — do not wait until care is otherwise complete
- [ ] Remove wet clothing if operationally safe and feasible
- [ ] Place insulation UNDER the casualty before anything else (ground conduction is the largest heat loss route)
- [ ] Wrap with space/mylar blanket, reflective side facing the body
- [ ] Cover the head and neck (up to 40-50% of heat loss is cephalic)
- [ ] Place chemical heat packs in axillae and groin — do NOT place directly on skin; wrap in a layer of clothing
- [ ] Seal the wrap to prevent convective losses from wind
- [ ] Warm IV fluids if in Prolonged Field Care and equipment is available
- [ ] Monitor for cardiac arrhythmia in severe hypothermia (below 28C / 82.4F)
- [ ] Treat gently — cold hearts are prone to ventricular fibrillation with rough handling
