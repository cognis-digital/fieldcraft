# Environmental survival

> **Disclaimer:** Educational / reference / preparedness material with links to public and open-source resources. Not operational instructions for violence or weapons. Verify against primary sources; train with qualified instructors. Follow all applicable laws.

Environmental survival and SERE (Survival, Evasion, Resistance, Escape)-adjacent education covers recognition, prevention, and field management of injuries caused by extreme environmental conditions — cold, heat, altitude, water/marine, and desert. This knowledge base draws on US Army field manuals (FM 21-76, TC 4-02.1, ATP 4-02.11, FM 31-70), Wilderness Medical Society clinical guidelines, CDC/FEMA public health resources, and NOLS/wilderness medicine curricula. The domain applies equally to military personnel, emergency responders, wilderness recreationists, and civilians preparing for extreme environments. Core principle: prevention is always preferred over treatment; recognize early warning signs before a condition becomes life-threatening.

## Cold-Weather Injuries: Hypothermia

Hypothermia occurs when core body temperature drops below 95°F (35°C). It is the leading killer in cold environments and can occur at temperatures well above freezing when wind, wetness, or exhaustion are present.

**Classification by core temperature:**
- Mild: 90–95°F (32–35°C) — shivering (the body's last active defense), confusion, slurred speech, clumsy movements.
- Moderate: 82–90°F (28–32°C) — shivering stops (dangerous sign), increasing confusion, muscle rigidity, paradoxical undressing behavior possible.
- Severe: below 82°F (28°C) — unconsciousness, cardiac arrhythmia, pulses difficult to detect. "No one is dead until they are warm and dead" — resuscitation attempts warranted until hospital rewarming confirms death.

**Field Recognition:** Use the "Umbles" mnemonic — stumbles, mumbles, fumbles, grumbles. Any of these in a cold environment demands immediate action.

**Prevention (ECWCS COLD principle):**
- C: Keep clothing Clean (dirt/oil reduce insulation)
- O: Avoid Overheating (sweat = moisture = heat loss)
- L: Layer clothing (base/mid/shell system)
- D: Keep clothing Dry

**Field Treatment:**
1. Remove from wind/cold into best available shelter.
2. Replace wet clothing with dry; insulate from ground (ground conduction accelerates heat loss).
3. For mild cases: warm sweet drinks if conscious, warm the core (not the extremities first — "after-drop" risk).
4. Do NOT rewarm rapidly — sudden vasodilation can cause circulatory collapse and cardiac arrest.
5. Handle gently — rough movement can trigger ventricular fibrillation in moderate/severe cases.
6. Evacuate urgently; definitive treatment requires hospital active rewarming.

**Wind Chill Factor:** Wind chill describes the perceived temperature due to combined cold air and wind speed. At 0°F with 15 mph wind, wind chill reaches −19°F; exposed skin can freeze in 30 minutes. At −35°F wind chill, frostbite can occur in under 10 minutes. Wind chill cannot cause frostbite if the actual air temperature is above 32°F — but hypothermia remains possible. NOAA Wind Chill Chart: https://www.weather.gov/safety/cold-wind-chill-chart

**Sources:** TC 21-3 Soldier's Handbook; FM 21-76 US Army Survival Manual; DTIC ADA395745 (Sustaining Health and Performance in Cold).

## Cold-Weather Injuries: Frostbite, Frostnip, and Trench Foot

**Frostnip** is the precursor injury — superficial skin freezing with ice crystals on surface (typically cheeks, ears, nose). Skin is red, cold, painful. Rewarm immediately by covering with warm hands; never rub. Frostnip signals that frostbite is imminent if conditions continue.

**Frostbite Classification (WMS 2-tier field system):**
- Superficial (corresponds to 1st/2nd degree): Skin is firm, pale, waxy, numb. After rewarming: redness, swelling, clear or milky blisters within 24–48 hours. No anticipated tissue loss.
- Deep (corresponds to 3rd/4th degree): Tissue is hard, wooden, involves subcutaneous tissue, muscle, or bone. After rewarming: hemorrhagic (blood-filled) blisters, eschar (black tissue) develops. Anticipated tissue loss. Classic 4-degree system exists but is difficult to apply in field before rewarming.

**Field Rewarming Protocol (WMS 2024 Guidelines):**
- Only rewarm in field if you can guarantee the tissue will STAY thawed. Refreeze-thaw cycles cause catastrophically worse injury than delayed rewarming.
- Rapidly rewarm in water maintained at 37–39°C (98.6–102.2°F) for approximately 30 minutes until tissue is soft and pliable.
- Give NSAIDs (ibuprofen) for pain and their anti-inflammatory/anti-thromboxane effect; opioids for severe pain as available.
- Do NOT rub frostbitten tissue — ice crystals lacerate cells.
- Do NOT break blisters unless necessary for function; they form a protective environment.
- Elevate to reduce edema. Evacuate to definitive care.

**Trench Foot / Non-Freezing Cold Injury (NFCI):** Caused by prolonged exposure to cold (above freezing, typically 0–15°C), wet conditions. Does NOT require freezing. Historically devastating in WWI and remains a military training hazard. Four stages: cold exposure, prehyperaemic (cold/numb/pale), hyperaemic (rewarming pain, red, swollen), posthyperaemic. Treatment: slow gradual rewarming (NOT rapid), keep extremities cool while raising core temperature, amitriptyline is most effective for neuropathic pain. Prevention: daily sock changes, foot inspection, keep feet as dry as possible.

**High-Risk Body Parts:** Fingers, toes, ears, nose, cheeks, chin, penis.

**Sources:** WMS Clinical Practice Guidelines for Frostbite 2024 (Sage Journals); WMS NFCI Supplement 2023; North American Rescue WMS Frostbite Guide.

## Heat Injuries: Recognition and Treatment

Heat injuries occur on a spectrum from minor heat cramps to life-threatening heat stroke. The military uses the Wet Bulb Globe Temperature (WBGT) index to quantify heat stress, accounting for temperature, humidity, wind, and solar radiation — more accurate than air temperature alone.

**WBGT Heat Categories (US Army):**
- White (<78°F WBGT): Normal activity
- Green (78–81.9°F): Limit strenuous exercise for unacclimatized personnel
- Yellow (82–84.9°F): Rest 10 min/hour for unacclimatized
- Red (85–87.9°F): Limit physical training; rest 20 min/hour
- Black (>88°F): Suspend all non-essential activity

**Correction factors:** Add 5°F WBGT when wearing rucksack/body armor; add 10°F in full MOPP/chemical protective gear.

**Heat Cramps:** Painful muscle spasms (legs, abdomen) from electrolyte depletion. Not life-threatening. Treatment: move to cool area, gentle stretching, 0.5 glass cool water or sports drink every 15 minutes.

**Heat Exhaustion:** Core temp elevated but below 104°F (40°C). Signs: heavy sweating, cool/pale/moist skin, weakness, dizziness, nausea, headache, fast/weak pulse. Treatment: move to cool shade, remove tight/outer clothing, cool with wet cloths/spray/fan, oral fluids if conscious. Elevate legs. Monitor for heat stroke progression.

**Heat Stroke (MEDICAL EMERGENCY):** Core temp above 104°F (40°C) PLUS central nervous system dysfunction (confusion, combativeness, seizure, loss of consciousness). Two types:
- Classic (non-exertional): elderly, chronically ill, hot environments, no sweating
- Exertional: healthy, active individuals (soldiers, athletes) — may still be sweating

Treatment — COLD WATER IMMERSION is gold standard:
1. Call 911 / activate medevac immediately.
2. Remove clothing; immerse in cold/ice water up to neck if available.
3. If immersion unavailable: apply ice packs to neck, armpits, groin (high blood-flow areas); fan with cool mist.
4. Do NOT give fluids by mouth to unconscious casualty.
5. Continue cooling for 20 minutes or until EMS arrives.
6. Every minute of delay in cooling increases organ damage risk — "cool first, transport second."

**Hydration Guidelines:** Drink 0.5–1 quart (500 mL–1 L) per hour during moderate activity; 1–1.5 quarts during intense activity. Do NOT exceed 1.5 quarts/hour — hyponatremia (water intoxication) is a real risk. Electrolyte replacement matters during prolonged activity. Urine color is a useful field indicator: pale yellow = adequate; dark amber = dehydrated.

**Acclimatization:** Takes 10–14 days of progressive heat exposure. Begin before deploying to hot environments. Acclimatized individuals have higher sweat volume, lower core temp, lower heart rate for same workload.

**Sources:** Army Heat Illness Prevention & Sun Safety (ph.health.mil); TRADOC TR350-29; Red Cross Heat Stroke First Aid; CDC Heat Illnesses (wwwnc.cdc.gov).

## Altitude Illness: AMS, HACE, and HAPE

Altitude illness results when ascent outpaces physiological acclimatization to reduced atmospheric oxygen (hypoxia). Problems typically begin above 8,000 feet (2,440 m), though individuals vary significantly in susceptibility.

**Acute Mountain Sickness (AMS):** Most common form; affects over 25% of people at elevations above 6,000 ft. Develops within 8–24 hours of arriving at altitude. Diagnosed using the Lake Louise Score.
- Symptoms: persistent headache (cardinal symptom), nausea, vomiting, fatigue, poor appetite, dizziness, insomnia.
- Treatment: halt ascent and rest; NSAIDs/acetaminophen for headache; acetazolamide (Diamox) 125–250 mg twice daily speeds acclimatization; descend 300–500 m if worsening.

**High Altitude Cerebral Edema (HACE):** Severe brain swelling; AMS progressing to neurological dysfunction. Typically above 9,000 ft (2,750 m).
- Symptoms: severe headache unresponsive to analgesics, ataxia (loss of coordination — perform tandem gait test), altered mental status, confusion, lethargy, coma.
- Can be fatal within hours if untreated.
- Treatment: IMMEDIATE DESCENT (most critical intervention) by at least 300–1,000 m. Supplemental oxygen (2–4 L/min). Dexamethasone 8 mg loading dose then 4 mg every 6 hours. Gamow bag (portable hyperbaric chamber) if descent impossible.

**High Altitude Pulmonary Edema (HAPE):** Fluid accumulation in lungs; most common cause of altitude-related death. Can occur without prior AMS.
- Symptoms: dry cough progressing to productive cough with pink/frothy sputum, dyspnea at rest, cyanosis, rapid heart rate, extreme fatigue.
- Can kill within 40 hours of onset.
- Treatment: IMMEDIATE DESCENT (at least 300–1,000 m — first priority even above oxygen). High-flow supplemental oxygen (4–6 L/min). Nifedipine 30 mg extended-release if descent delayed. Gamow bag as bridge. Strict rest.

**Prevention:**
- Ascend gradually: above 3,000 m (9,840 ft), limit sleeping altitude gain to 300–500 m/day.
- "Climb high, sleep low" — acclimatize at altitude, return to lower elevation to sleep.
- Rest day every 1,000 m gained above 3,000 m.
- Acetazolamide 125–250 mg twice daily starting 1–2 days before ascent (prescription required; avoid if sulfa allergy).
- Avoid alcohol and sedatives at altitude (suppress respiratory drive).
- Previous susceptibility is a strong predictor; know your history.

**Gamow Bag:** Portable hyperbaric chamber weighing ~6 kg that raises effective altitude equivalent by 1,500–2,500 m. Field emergency tool only — not a substitute for descent.

**Sources:** CDC Yellow Book 2024; WildsafeOrg Altitude Illnesses 101; AAFP/AFP 2020 WMS Guidelines; NIH/NCBI StatPearls AMS.

## Water and Marine Survival

Cold water immersion is one of the most rapidly lethal environmental emergencies: water conducts heat 25 times faster than air at the same temperature. Survival without a PFD (personal flotation device) is measured in minutes in near-freezing water.

**The Cold Water 1-10-1 Principle (Gordon Giesbrecht, University of Manitoba):**
- 1 minute: Cold shock response — involuntary gasp, hyperventilation, cardiovascular stress. DO NOT PANIC. Control breathing. This passes in ~1 minute.
- 10 minutes: Cold incapacitation — swimming failure as muscles cool. You cannot voluntarily swim for long. Focus on self-rescue immediately.
- 1 hour: Hypothermia — loss of consciousness. (Highly variable by body mass, clothing, water temp.)

**Survival Priorities in Water:**
1. Do not remove clothing — clothing traps water that warms to body temp ("wet suit effect").
2. Get out of the water as fast as possible — even on an ice shelf or capsized hull.
3. If rescue is coming, conserve heat: assume H.E.L.P. position.
4. If multiple survivors: form a huddle (chests together, arms around each other).

**H.E.L.P. Position (Heat Escape Lessening Posture):** Cross arms over chest, draw knees up to chest, keep head out of water. Reduces heat loss by protecting groin, armpits, and chest — the body's three high heat-loss zones. Only effective with a PFD (life jacket) keeping you afloat.

**Huddle Formation:** Groups of 2–4 people face inward, chests together, arms around each other. Slows heat loss and makes the group more visible to rescuers.

**Treatment of Cold Water Immersion Casualty:**
- Handle GENTLY — cold heart is prone to arrhythmia.
- Horizontal position; do not make patient stand or walk (orthostatic collapse risk).
- Remove wet clothing; insulate from ground up.
- Treat as for hypothermia (see Cold-Weather section).
- Begin CPR if pulseless — cold cardiac arrest is survivable; continue until patient is rewarmed.

**Field Water Purification (Drinking Water Safety):**
All surface water should be considered contaminated. Methods by reliability:
1. **Boiling**: Most reliable. Bring to a rolling boil 1–3 minutes (3 min above 6,500 ft altitude). Kills bacteria, viruses, protozoa.
2. **Chemical (Iodine/Chlorine)**: Tablets or drops; wait 30 min (longer for cold/turbid water). Effective against bacteria and viruses; less effective against Cryptosporidium. Iodine not suitable for pregnant women or thyroid conditions; long-term use cautioned.
3. **Filtration**: Ceramic/hollow-fiber filters (e.g., Sawyer, MSR) remove protozoa and bacteria but do NOT remove viruses — pair with chemical treatment in regions with high virus risk.
4. **UV (SteriPen-type)**: Destroys DNA of all pathogens including viruses; ineffective in turbid water (pre-filter first).
5. **SODIS (Solar Disinfection)**: Clear PET bottles + 6+ hours direct sunlight. Emergency backup only.

**Sources:** Coast Guard Cold Water Survival bulletin; Minnesota DNR Cold Water Dangers; FM 21-76 Chapter on water procurement; Adventure Medical Kits Cold Water Immersion.

## Desert Survival: Heat, Dehydration, and Sandstorms

Deserts combine extreme heat, solar radiation, low humidity, scarce water, and sudden temperature extremes (temperatures can swing 50°F between day and night). Dehydration is the primary killer; heat stroke and hypothermia (night/winter desert) are secondary threats.

**Dehydration Recognition and Stages:**
- 1–2% body water loss: Thirst, slightly decreased performance.
- 5% loss: Irritability, nausea, weakness, headache, dark urine, reduced judgment.
- 8–10% loss: Dizziness, confusion, inability to walk steadily, extreme weakness.
- 15%+ loss: Death.

Field test: skin pinch test — skin tenting (slow return to normal) indicates significant dehydration. Urine color — pale yellow is target; dark amber/orange = dehydrated; no urine = critically dehydrated.

**Water Requirements:**
- At moderate exertion in 68°F (20°C): 2–3 liters/day minimum.
- In desert at 100°F+ with physical activity: up to 1 liter per hour.
- Drink at regular intervals; DO NOT wait for thirst — thirst is a lagging indicator.
- DO NOT ration water if rescue is expected soon — drink what you need.
- Electrolyte replacement: mix 0.25 teaspoon salt per liter; sports drinks acceptable.

**Desert Survival Priorities (FM 21-76):**
1. Water — find/carry/protect; this overrides all other needs.
2. Shelter — from solar radiation and wind, not just from precipitation.
3. Signaling — conserve energy, do not exhaust yourself in midday heat.
4. Food — secondary; humans can survive weeks without food, days without water.

**Desert Heat Management:**
- Travel at night or early morning; rest in shade 10:00–15:00.
- Ground temperature can be 30–40°F hotter than air temperature — sit on something, not directly on sand/rock.
- Wear loose, light-colored, full-coverage clothing (reduces radiant heat gain and sweat evaporation rate — counterintuitive but effective).
- Head cover is mandatory — the head radiates significant heat.
- Seek shade whenever stationary; improvise from vehicle, parachute, poncho, terrain.

**Desert Hypothermia (often overlooked):** After sunset in high deserts, temperatures drop rapidly. Soldiers/survivors who sweat heavily during the day become chilled at night. Wet clothing from sweat combined with wind creates the same hypothermia risk as winter environments. Carry insulating layers even in summer desert operations.

**Sandstorms:** Visibility drops to zero within seconds; dust inhalation causes respiratory damage. Actions: seek terrain shelter (not open ground), mark your location, protect airways with wet cloth or respirator, power off electronics and cover with wrap, do not drive if storm approaches.

**Sources:** FM 21-76 US Army Survival Manual (archive.org); FM 90-3 Desert Operations; BITS.de FM 21-76 archive.

## Layering Systems and Clothing-Based Heat Regulation

Proper clothing management is the primary preventive tool for both cold and heat injuries. The military's Extended Cold Weather Clothing System (ECWCS) Gen III is the current US Army standard and represents the principles applicable to any cold environment.

**The Three-Layer Principle:**

**Base Layer (Moisture Management):**
- Purpose: Wick moisture (sweat) away from skin.
- Materials: Synthetic (polyester, polypropylene) or merino wool. NEVER cotton in cold environments — cotton absorbs moisture and loses all insulative value when wet. "Cotton kills."
- ECWCS Level I: Lightweight silkweight for mild cold/high activity.
- ECWCS Level II: Midweight for moderate cold/lower activity.

**Mid Layer (Insulation):**
- Purpose: Trap warm air close to body.
- Materials: Fleece, down, synthetic fill.
- ECWCS Level III: Polartec 300 fleece — breathable, quick-dry insulation.
- ECWCS Level IV: Wind-resistant soft shell — mid-layer protection for wind-exposed conditions.
- Rule: Insulation is only as effective as the base layer is dry. A wet insulation layer can become a liability.

**Outer Layer (Shell / Protection):**
- Purpose: Block wind, rain, snow while allowing moisture vapor to escape (breathability).
- Materials: GORE-TEX and comparable waterproof/breathable membranes.
- ECWCS Level VI: GORE-TEX Paclite — lightweight waterproof/breathable.
- ECWCS Level VII: Extreme cold weather parka/bib (down or synthetic fill + shell).

**ECWCS Temperature Range:** Gen III is rated from +40°F down to −60°F depending on which layers are worn in combination.

**Key Principles:**
- Ventilate before you sweat — open zippers, remove a layer before high exertion begins.
- Dress for the activity, not the standing temperature.
- Protect extremities separately: insulated gloves/mittens, gaiters, waterproof boots, balaclava.
- Mittens retain more warmth than gloves; use trigger-finger mitts for weapon handling.

**For Heat Environments:**
- Light-colored, loose, wicking fabrics reduce radiant absorption and enhance evaporative cooling.
- Head cover is critical (reduces sun load on the body's heat-sensing system).
- Body armor worn in heat: accounts for +5°F WBGT equivalent increase in heat stress.

**Sources:** ECWCS description (military.com); USAMM ECWCS Guide; TacticalGear.com Layering Guide.

## Field Assessment Framework and Evacuation Decision-Making

Environmental casualties require systematic assessment to prioritize treatment and make evacuation decisions. The framework used in wilderness/military medicine integrates standard trauma assessment with environmental-specific considerations.

**STOP Mnemonic (Survival Decision-Making):**
- S: Stop — halt movement, prevent panic.
- T: Think — assess your situation and resources.
- O: Observe — environment, hazards, potential resources.
- P: Plan — develop and execute a course of action.

**SAMPLE History for Environmental Casualties:**
- S: Symptoms and chief complaint
- A: Allergies (especially medication: sulfa drugs with acetazolamide; ibuprofen with certain cold injuries)
- M: Medications currently taken
- P: Pertinent past history (altitude sickness previously? cardiac history?)
- L: Last oral intake (fluids, food — critical for dehydration/hyponatremia assessment)
- E: Events leading to injury (duration of exposure, activity level, environmental conditions)

**Evacuation Urgency Tiers (Wilderness Medicine):**
- Emergent (go NOW, any means): Heat stroke, severe hypothermia (unconscious), HACE, HAPE, cold water drowning, suspected spinal injury.
- Urgent (evacuate within hours): Moderate hypothermia, deep frostbite, AMS worsening, significant dehydration with altered mental status.
- Non-urgent (can walk out): Mild AMS responding to rest, heat exhaustion responding to treatment, mild frostbite, heat cramps.

**Gamow Bag Decision Point:** If descent is impossible and patient has HACE or HAPE, deploy Gamow bag immediately. Pumping provides ~1 psi overpressure, equivalent to descending 1,500–2,500 m. Time in bag: 1–2 hours minimum. This is a bridge to descent, not a cure.

**Buddy System Imperative:** Most environmental casualties are preventable through the buddy system — mutual observation for early signs (shivering, stumbling, change in behavior, skin color change, slowing pace). Leaders must actively monitor all personnel in extreme environments, not rely on self-reporting.

**WBGT / Environmental Monitoring:** Field commanders should use WBGT meters in heat environments and wind chill index in cold to make evidence-based work/rest decisions. Both tools are publicly available via NOAA (weather.gov) and military public health resources.

**Sources:** FM 21-76; TC 4-02.1 (2016, Army Pubs); ATP 4-02.11 (2026, supersedes TC 4-02.1); NOLS Wilderness Medicine Core Curriculum; WMS Practice Guidelines.

### Checklist: Personal Cold Weather Gear Inspection (Before Mission/Activity)

- [ ] Base layer: moisture-wicking synthetic or wool — NO cotton
- [ ] Mid layer: insulating fleece or synthetic fill; dry and clean
- [ ] Outer shell: waterproof/breathable; seams intact
- [ ] Extremity protection: insulated gloves/mittens, wool/synthetic socks (carry a dry spare pair)
- [ ] Head/neck: balaclava or insulating hat covering ears; neck gaiter
- [ ] Boots: waterproof, insulated for expected temperature; broken in (no blisters)
- [ ] Hand and foot chemical warmers as backup
- [ ] Vapor barrier layer if expected temperature below −20°F
- [ ] Emergency bivouac material: space blanket, bivy sack, or sleeping bag
- [ ] High-calorie food and warm drink capability (vacuum flask)

### Checklist: Heat Injury Prevention Checklist (Daily Hot Weather Operations)

- [ ] Check WBGT index before starting operations; apply work/rest table
- [ ] Adjust WBGT: +5°F for rucksack or body armor; +10°F for full MOPP gear
- [ ] Verify all personnel have adequate water (minimum 0.5 qt/hour at moderate exertion)
- [ ] Do NOT exceed 1.5 qt/hour — hyponatremia risk
- [ ] Brief personnel on heat injury signs: confusion, stopping sweating, hot/dry red skin = EMERGENCY
- [ ] Buddy system: observe each other for stumbling, confusion, behavior change
- [ ] Identify cooling resources: shade, water source, cold water immersion capability
- [ ] Ensure high-heat casualty kit: ice packs or cold water container, spray bottle
- [ ] Acclimatization: do not conduct high-intensity operations with unacclimatized personnel in first 10–14 days
- [ ] Identify nearest medical facility and evacuation route before operations begin

### Checklist: Altitude Ascent Safety Checklist

- [ ] Know baseline susceptibility (previous AMS = higher risk)
- [ ] Plan ascent: above 3,000 m, limit sleeping altitude gain to 300–500 m/day
- [ ] Schedule a rest day every 1,000 m gained above 3,000 m
- [ ] Apply 'climb high, sleep low' when acclimatizing
- [ ] Carry acetazolamide (Diamox) if prescribed; begin 1–2 days before ascent
- [ ] No alcohol or sedatives at altitude
- [ ] Carry supplemental oxygen and/or Gamow bag for objectives above 4,000 m
- [ ] Carry dexamethasone (prescription) for HACE treatment
- [ ] Know and brief evacuation route before ascending
- [ ] Perform daily tandem gait test (straight-line walking) to screen for HACE
- [ ] Know HAPE warning signs: dry cough, dyspnea at rest, pink frothy sputum — DESCEND IMMEDIATELY

### Checklist: Frostbite/Hypothermia Field Treatment Quick Reference

- [ ] Remove casualty from cold/wind; get into best available shelter
- [ ] Replace all wet clothing with dry; insulate from ground (never place directly on cold ground)
- [ ] Handle gently — rough movement can trigger cardiac arrhythmia in moderate/severe hypothermia
- [ ] Warm sweet drinks ONLY if casualty is fully conscious and able to swallow
- [ ] For frostbitten extremity: do NOT rewarm unless guaranteed it will stay thawed
- [ ] Rewarm frostbite in 37–39°C water for ~30 minutes; do NOT rub or apply direct heat
- [ ] Give NSAIDs (ibuprofen) for frostbite pain and anti-thromboxane effect
- [ ] Do NOT break frostbite blisters unless necessary for function
- [ ] Do NOT give alcohol (accelerates heat loss)
- [ ] Evacuate all but the mildest cases to definitive medical care
- [ ] Severe hypothermia: begin CPR if pulseless — 'no one is dead until warm and dead'

### Checklist: Water Survival / Cold Water Immersion Response

- [ ] Always wear PFD when boating in cold water — you cannot don it after entering cold shock
- [ ] If immersed: control breathing for first 60 seconds — cold shock passes
- [ ] Attempt immediate self-rescue within first 10 minutes before muscle incapacitation
- [ ] If rescue is coming: assume H.E.L.P. position (arms crossed, knees up) if wearing PFD
- [ ] Multiple survivors: form huddle — chests together, arms around each other
- [ ] Do NOT remove clothing in the water — it provides insulation
- [ ] Once rescued: lie flat (horizontal), avoid standing (orthostatic collapse risk)
- [ ] Handle victim gently; remove wet clothing; insulate
- [ ] Begin CPR if no pulse — cold cardiac arrest is potentially survivable with rewarming
- [ ] Activate emergency services immediately; cold water casualties need hospital rewarming
