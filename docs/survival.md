# Survival fundamentals

> **Disclaimer:** Educational / reference / preparedness material with links to public and open-source resources. Not operational instructions for violence or weapons. Verify against primary sources; train with qualified instructors. Follow all applicable laws.

Core survival knowledge drawn from the US Army FM 21-76 / FM 3-05.70 Survival Manual, FEMA Ready.gov, the American Red Cross, NOLS Wilderness Medicine, and recognized outdoor education organizations. This reference covers the proven priorities of survival — the "Rule of Threes" framework — and practical skills for water procurement, shelter construction, fire-starting, signaling, food procurement, navigation, first aid, and survival psychology. The material is educational and preparedness-oriented, suitable for soldiers, first responders, hikers, and civilians. For any genuine emergency in the United States, call 911 and activate your local emergency plan.

## The Rule of Threes: Survival Priority Framework

The Rule of Threes is a widely-taught mnemonic that defines the order of priority in any survival situation by describing the approximate time a person can survive without each critical resource:

- 3 minutes without air (or in icy water)
- 3 hours without shelter in a harsh environment
- 3 days without water
- 3 weeks without food

This framework does not mean you wait 3 hours to build shelter; it means shelter takes priority over water, and water takes priority over food when triage decisions must be made. A fire is often considered a force-multiplier that supports all three: it provides warmth (shelter function), can purify water, and can prepare food. Signaling — making yourself findable to rescuers — is a parallel priority that should begin as early as possible. FM 21-76 reinforces this hierarchy throughout its chapters on survival planning.

IMPORTANT CAVEAT: The Rule of Threes is a heuristic, not a physical law. Real conditions vary enormously. In extreme cold, shelter may matter in minutes, not hours. In a desert, water depletion accelerates. In a rescue-likely scenario (downed aircraft, known search area), signaling outweighs all other priorities. Always adapt to your actual situation.

## Survival Psychology: The STOP Method and the Will to Survive

FM 21-76 opens with a chapter on the psychology of survival because panic is the deadliest initial enemy. The US Army identifies fear, anxiety, confusion, hunger, thirst, fatigue, loneliness, and boredom as the key stressors that degrade decision-making.

The STOP method is a widely-taught first response to finding yourself in a survival situation:

- S — Stop: Halt movement immediately. Continued movement when disoriented accelerates the problem.
- T — Think: Use deliberate reasoning. What do you know? What resources do you have? What is your last known position?
- O — Observe: Assess your environment. What is the terrain? What is the weather doing? What materials are available?
- P — Plan: Develop a prioritized action sequence. Address immediate threats first (hypothermia risk, injury), then address the Rule of Threes hierarchy.

The survival mindset — sometimes called the 'will to survive' — is considered by military doctrine to be as important as any physical skill. Personnel who have survived extreme situations consistently report that mental determination, a reason to live, and refusing to catastrophize were decisive factors. Training reduces fear by building competence; regular practice of these skills under low-stakes conditions is the best preparation.

## Water: Procurement and Purification

Water is the second priority after immediate shelter/thermal protection. Dehydration degrades cognitive function rapidly, compromising every other survival decision.

FINDING WATER:
- Follow terrain downhill; water flows to valleys, ravines, and drainage channels.
- Watch animal trails, which often lead to water sources.
- Look for dense vegetation, willows, cottonwoods, and cattails as indicators.
- Collect rainwater using any impermeable sheet (tarp, poncho, emergency blanket, large leaves).
- Build a solar still: dig a hole approximately 3 feet wide and 2 feet deep, place a container at the center, cover the hole with clear plastic sheeting, seal the edges with soil, and place a small rock in the center to create a low point over the container. Condensation collects and drips into the container. Yield is modest (up to 1 liter per day in favorable conditions).
- In cold environments, melt snow or ice — never eat snow directly, as it lowers core body temperature.

NEVER drink: seawater, urine, blood, or unknown plant fluids as hydration sources — these accelerate dehydration or cause illness.

PURIFICATION — all natural water should be treated before drinking:
1. Boiling: The most reliable field method. Bring water to a rolling boil for 1 minute (add 1 minute per 1,000 feet above sea level, or simply boil 3 minutes at any altitude to be safe). Kills bacteria, viruses, and protozoa.
2. Chemical treatment (iodine): 5 drops of 2% tincture of iodine per liter of clear water; 10 drops for cloudy or cold water. Wait 30 minutes before drinking. Less effective against Cryptosporidium. Not suitable for pregnant women or thyroid patients.
3. Chlorine tablets (sodium hypochlorite/calcium hypochlorite): Follow manufacturer instructions. Effective against bacteria and viruses; less effective against Cryptosporidium.
4. Filtration + purification: Field filters (ceramic, hollow-fiber) remove protozoa and bacteria but not viruses; always combine with chemical treatment or boiling for full protection in backcountry use.
5. UV purifiers (SteriPen and equivalents): Effective against all pathogens when water is clear; battery-dependent.

## Shelter: Construction and Thermal Protection

Shelter is often the highest priority because hypothermia (core temperature drop) can kill in less than 3 hours in wet and cold conditions, even at temperatures well above freezing. Shelter also includes protection from radiant heat and solar radiation in desert environments.

KEY PRINCIPLE: The goal of a field shelter is to create a small, insulated air space around your body. Smaller is warmer. Dead air space (provided by dry debris, leaves, or insulating layers) is your primary insulating material in a no-kit situation.

COMMON IMPROVISED SHELTER TYPES:

1. Debris Hut: Best no-gear option in forested terrain. Construction: lean a long ridgepole (8-12 feet) against a fork or stump at about hip height, creating an A-frame skeleton. Fill in the sides with sticks angled against the ridgepole. Pile at least 2-3 feet of dry leaves, pine needles, ferns, or forest duff over the entire structure. The inside should be just wide and tall enough to lie down — extra space is heat lost. Fill the interior with dry debris as a 'sleeping bag' of leaves.

2. Lean-to: Fast to build with a tarp, poncho, or mylar emergency blanket. Tie a ridgeline between two trees at waist-to-chest height. Drape the tarp over it, stake down the back edge close to the ground, and angle it away from the wind. Add side walls for additional wind protection. Reflects a fire's heat back toward occupants effectively.

3. Tarp A-frame: Fold a tarp over a ridgeline, stake both long edges to the ground. Provides full enclosure. Fast in rain. Combine with a ground sheet to block conductive heat loss from the cold ground — ground conduction is a major cause of hypothermia.

4. Snow shelters (Quinzee / snow trench): In winter survival, snow is a remarkable insulator. A snow trench covered with branches and snow keeps occupants well above ambient temperature. A quinzee (hollowed snow mound, walls at least 6 inches thick) can maintain interior temperatures near 32°F even when outside temperatures are -40°F.

GROUND INSULATION: Always prioritize insulation beneath you. More heat is lost to conductive transfer through the ground than through air. At minimum, use 3-4 inches of dry debris between your body and the earth.

SITE SELECTION:
- Avoid valley floors (cold air pooling, flooding risk)
- Avoid ridge tops (exposed to wind)
- Avoid dry riverbeds or washes (flash flooding)
- Seek natural windbreaks: rock faces, dense evergreens
- Be aware of widow-makers (dead overhead branches) and slope hazards

## Fire: Starting, Maintaining, and Using Fire in Survival

Fire serves multiple survival functions: thermal regulation, water purification, food preparation, morale, insect repellent, and signaling. In cold conditions, fire may represent the difference between life and death.

THE FIRE TRIANGLE: Every fire requires heat, fuel, and oxygen. Removing any one extinguishes the fire.

FUEL HIERARCHY — always prepare materials before attempting ignition:
1. Tinder: Fine, dry, easily-ignited material with a high surface-area-to-mass ratio. Natural examples: dead dry grass, cattail fluff, pine needle duff, birch bark (inner layer), dried fungi, finely-shredded dry bark. Commercial examples: petroleum jelly-coated cotton balls, wax-based fire starters, char cloth. Fatwood (resin-saturated dead pine heartwood) is an exceptionally reliable natural tinder.
2. Kindling: Dry twigs approximately pencil-width. Gradually increase size from matchstick diameter to pencil diameter to finger diameter.
3. Fuel: Larger branches and logs. Hardwoods produce longer-lasting coals; softwoods ignite more easily.

FIRE-STARTING METHODS:

With modern tools (carry at least two ignition sources):
- Lighter: Most reliable; carry backup fuel
- Ferrocerium (ferro) rod: Produces sparks at ~5,000°F; waterproof and very long-lasting
- Waterproof matches
- Magnesium block with integrated striker

Primitive friction fire (requires practice BEFORE an emergency):
- Bow drill: A wooden spindle is rotated at high speed against a fireboard using a bow. Friction generates a coal in the fireboard's notch. The coal is transferred to a tinder bundle and blown into flame. Requires matched dry wood (soft varieties: willow, cottonwood, basswood, mullein). This is genuinely difficult without prior practice.
- Hand drill: Similar principle without the bow; requires very dry conditions and harder physical effort.
- Fire plow: A hardwood peg dragged along a groove in a softwood board; slower but simpler to construct.

FIRE LAYS (common structures):
- Teepee lay: Tinder at center, kindling leaned over it in a cone. Best for fast ignition.
- Log cabin lay: Log frame around tinder/kindling. Burns long, produces good coals for cooking.
- Star fire: Large logs arranged like spokes; push inward as they burn. Conserves fuel.

SAFETY: Clear a minimum 10-foot radius of all flammable material before lighting. Never leave a fire unattended. Fully extinguish with water and stir ash until cool to the touch before leaving a site.

## Signaling: Making Yourself Found

In most civilian survival scenarios, rescue is more reliable than self-rescue. Signaling should begin as early as practical and be sustained. The fundamental rule: make yourself visible, audible, and distinct from the natural environment.

UNIVERSAL DISTRESS SIGNAL: Any group of three (three fires, three blasts of a whistle, three flashes of light, three shouts) is internationally recognized as a distress signal. Repeat at regular intervals.

SOS: The Morse code distress signal (three short / three long / three short: · · · — — — · · ·) is internationally recognized both in radio and in visual flashing signals. Lay it out in rocks, logs, or trampled vegetation on open ground for aerial detection.

SIGNALING METHODS BY PRIORITY:

1. Electronic: A PLB (Personal Locator Beacon) or EPIRB transmits a GPS-coded distress signal to the COSPAS-SARSAT satellite system. Activation immediately alerts search-and-rescue. A registered SPOT or Garmin inReach device sends GPS position to a monitoring center. These are far more effective than any improvised method and should be part of any serious backcountry kit.

2. Signal mirror: Reflected sunlight can be seen for 10+ miles in clear conditions and is visible to aircraft at altitude. Standard technique with an aimed mirror: hold the mirror near your eye, create a sight by extending your other hand with fingers spread to frame the target (aircraft or person), tilt the mirror until reflected light passes through your fingers onto the target. Improvised: any reflective surface (CD, foil, metal) can reflect sunlight toward a target.

3. Whistle: A loud pea-less emergency whistle (Fox 40, Storm, or similar) produces a sound carrying far further than the human voice with far less energy expenditure. Three blasts = distress. Blow at regular intervals.

4. Signal fire and smoke: Three fires arranged in a triangle or in a line spaced approximately 100 feet apart is an international ground-to-air distress signal. To maximize visibility: in daylight, produce dark smoke (add rubber, plastic, green vegetation — use only materials safe to burn); at night, a bright flame is the signal. Place fires in clearings, hilltops, or lakeshores — not under dense forest canopy.

5. Ground-to-air symbols: Stomp, trench, or construct with contrast material (rocks, logs, dark debris on snow, or bright material on dark ground) the international symbols:
- X = require medical assistance
- V = require help
- → = traveling in this direction
- LL = all is well
Make each symbol at least 10 feet tall for aerial visibility.

6. Audible signals: Shout, bang metal, use a car horn if available. Sound travels further across water and at night.

If a rescue aircraft responds, the internationally-accepted acknowledgment is wing-rocking (day) or flashing lights (night). Do not move from your signaling location unless absolutely necessary — SAR teams return to last known position.

## Food: Procurement Basics and the Edibility Test

Food is the lowest priority in the survival hierarchy. A healthy adult can survive 3 weeks without food; mental and physical performance decline before that but not catastrophically in the short term. Spending excessive energy hunting or foraging while dehydrated or hypothermic is a common survival mistake.

FOOD PROCUREMENT OVERVIEW (FM 21-76, Chapters 8-9):

ANIMAL PROTEIN:
- Insects: The most calorie-efficient food source per unit of effort. Grubs, grasshoppers, ants, and earthworms are widely edible (remove wings and legs from large insects; cook when possible). Avoid brightly-colored insects and insects with strong odors (chemical defenses).
- Fish: Improvise hooks from safety pins, wire, or carved bone/wood. Weave gill nets from paracord or cordage. Still-water fishing with a suspended line requires minimal active effort. Clean and cook fish promptly.
- Small mammals and birds: Snares are more energy-efficient than active hunting. A simple loop snare from wire or cordage set on a game trail can take rabbits and small rodents. The figure-four deadfall trap is a classic improvised trap. FM 21-76 provides detailed construction instructions for these.
- Reptiles and amphibians: Generally edible when cooked; avoid brightly-colored frogs (poison dart varieties).

PLANT FOODS:
Foraging requires identification skill. The Universal Edibility Test (UEF), documented in FM 21-76, provides a step-by-step protocol for testing an unknown plant when no reference is available:
1. Test only one plant part (leaf, root, seed) at a time.
2. Fast for 8 hours.
3. Rub the plant part on the inside of the elbow or wrist; wait 15 minutes for contact reaction.
4. Hold a small piece against the lips for 3 minutes; check for burning/itching.
5. Place on tongue for 15 minutes without chewing; check reaction.
6. Chew thoroughly; wait 15 minutes without swallowing.
7. Swallow; wait 8 hours monitoring for nausea, cramps, or other reactions.
8. If no reaction, eat a slightly larger quantity and wait another 8 hours.

AVOID: Plants with milky or discolored sap, spines or fine hairs, a bean/pea-like appearance, bitter or soapy taste, grain heads with black/pink/purple spurs (ergot fungus), and an almond-like odor in woody parts (cyanide indicator).

Note: The Universal Edibility Test is a last-resort procedure. Wherever possible, use a verified regional field guide or knowledge of identifiable safe species (dandelion, cattail, pine inner bark, acorns after leaching) rather than testing unknowns.

## Navigation: Land Navigation Without Electronics

Navigation preserves the option to self-rescue when signaling fails or rescue is not expected. The primary tools are map, compass, and terrain association. GPS electronics are valuable but battery-dependent; always carry backup skills.

MAP AND COMPASS BASICS:
- Topographic maps show terrain shape via contour lines (each line = constant elevation; closely-spaced lines = steep terrain).
- Orient the map to terrain by aligning map features with observed landmarks.
- A baseplate compass (e.g., Suunto A-10, Silva Ranger) allows bearing measurement, declination adjustment, and pace-count distance estimation.
- Magnetic declination: The difference between magnetic north (compass) and true north (map grid) varies by location and must be accounted for. In the contiguous US, this ranges from approximately -20° (Pacific Northwest) to +20° (Maine). Declination values are printed on USGS topographic maps.

DEAD RECKONING: Navigate from a known point by maintaining a compass bearing and counting paces to estimate distance traveled. A 'pace count' is the number of double-steps (left foot strikes) per 100 meters; average is approximately 62-66 double-steps per 100m on level ground. Adjust for slope, terrain, and load.

TERRAIN ASSOCIATION: The primary skill of experienced navigators. Continuously compare visible terrain features (ridges, drainages, saddles, peaks, vegetation changes) to the map to maintain positional awareness without relying solely on pace counts.

PRIMITIVE / NO-TOOL NAVIGATION:
- Sun: In the Northern Hemisphere, the sun rises in the east, is due south at solar noon (highest point), and sets in the west.
- Shadow stick method: Place a stick vertically in the ground; mark the tip of its shadow. Wait 15-20 minutes; mark the new tip. A line drawn between the two marks runs approximately west to east (first mark = west, second = east). Note: only accurate near the equinoxes; introduces error at other times.
- North Star (Polaris): Find the Big Dipper's two 'pointer stars' (the outer edge of the cup); follow their direction approximately 5 times the distance between them to the North Star. Polaris sits within 1 degree of true north and does not rise or set appreciably.
- MYTH: Moss does not reliably grow on the north side of trees. Moisture, shade, and animal traffic influence moss growth on all sides. Do not navigate by moss.

When truly lost:
1. Apply STOP.
2. If in a search area, stay put and signal — this is almost always better than attempting self-rescue in unknown terrain.
3. If movement is necessary, follow water downhill: streams merge into rivers, rivers reach inhabited areas and roads.

## Wilderness First Aid Essentials: TCCC, Stop the Bleed, and Evacuation

Injuries in a survival situation demand field medicine. The following represents widely-taught civilian educational content — not a substitute for formal training.

THE PATIENT ASSESSMENT SYSTEM (NOLS/Red Cross WFA framework):
1. Scene safety: Is the scene safe to enter? Is there ongoing danger?
2. Mechanism of injury / illness: What happened?
3. Initial assessment (ABCs): Airway — is it open? Breathing — present and adequate? Circulation — significant bleeding?
4. Focused exam: Assess injuries from head to toe.
5. Vital signs and patient history (SAMPLE: Symptoms, Allergies, Medications, Past medical history, Last intake, Events).
6. Problem list and treatment plan.
7. Evacuation decision.

STOP THE BLEED (civilian hemorrhage control, stopthebleed.org):
Severe bleeding is the most preventable cause of death from traumatic injury. The three core techniques:
1. Direct pressure: Apply firm, sustained pressure directly to the wound using a cloth or dressing. Do not release to check — maintain pressure for at least 10 minutes.
2. Wound packing: For deep wounds (junctional wounds to neck, groin, armpit where tourniquets cannot be applied): pack the wound cavity firmly with gauze (hemostatic gauze such as QuikClot or Combat Gauze preferred), pressing material directly against the bleeding vessel, then apply sustained direct pressure for at least 3 minutes.
3. Tourniquet: For life-threatening bleeding from an extremity (arm or leg). Apply 2-3 inches above the wound (not over a joint). Tighten until bleeding stops. Note application time. A CAT (Combat Application Tourniquet) or SOFTT-W are the recommended commercial devices. Improvised tourniquets (wide cloth + windlass stick) are a last resort.

HYPOTHERMIA TREATMENT: Remove wet clothing. Insulate from ground and air. Provide gentle warmth (chemical heat packs to the torso core, armpits, groin — not extremities). Warm sweet fluids if the patient is conscious and can swallow. Handle gently — cardiac arrhythmia risk. Do not rub extremities.

HEAT ILLNESS:
- Heat exhaustion: Move to shade, cool environment; rest; oral hydration if conscious.
- Heat stroke (hot, dry skin; altered mental status): Medical emergency. Aggressive whole-body cooling (cool water immersion or wet towels with fanning) while evacuating.

EVACUATION CRITERIA: Evacuate immediately for: unconsciousness or altered mental status, difficulty breathing, chest pain, uncontrolled bleeding, suspected spinal injury, signs of shock (pale/cool/clammy skin, rapid weak pulse, altered consciousness), severe allergic reaction, severe burns, fractures with neurovascular compromise.

Formal WFA and WFR certifications are available through NOLS Wilderness Medicine and the American Red Cross.

### Checklist: 72-Hour Emergency Go-Bag (FEMA / Ready.gov Baseline)

- [ ] Water: 1 gallon per person per day (3-day minimum = 3 gallons/person); purification tablets or filter as backup
- [ ] Food: 3-day supply non-perishable (no-cook options preferred: energy bars, nuts, jerky, canned goods with pop-tops)
- [ ] Battery or hand-crank NOAA weather radio
- [ ] Flashlight with spare batteries (or headlamp)
- [ ] First aid kit (include bleed-control: tourniquet, pressure bandage, hemostatic gauze)
- [ ] Whistle for signaling
- [ ] Dust mask / N95 respirator
- [ ] Plastic sheeting and duct tape (shelter-in-place capability)
- [ ] Moist towelettes and hand sanitizer (sanitation)
- [ ] Garbage bags and zip ties
- [ ] Non-sparking wrench or pliers (utility shutoff)
- [ ] Manual can opener
- [ ] Local paper maps (phones and GPS fail)
- [ ] Cell phone with chargers and backup power bank
- [ ] Waterproof container with copies of vital documents (ID, insurance, contacts, prescriptions)
- [ ] Cash in small bills
- [ ] Prescription medications (7-day supply)
- [ ] Extra glasses or contact lens supplies if needed
- [ ] Infant or pet supplies if applicable
- [ ] Emergency mylar blankets (one per person)
- [ ] Change of clothing and sturdy closed-toe shoes per person

### Checklist: Backcountry / Wilderness Survival Kit (Ten Essentials Framework)

- [ ] Navigation: Topographic map of the area + baseplate compass (declination adjusted); GPS optional
- [ ] Sun protection: Sunscreen SPF 30+, sunglasses, wide-brim hat
- [ ] Insulation: Extra layers for the worst-case weather scenario; rain gear
- [ ] Illumination: Headlamp with fresh batteries + spare batteries
- [ ] First aid kit: Bleed control items, moleskin, SAM splint, irrigation syringe, WFA manual
- [ ] Fire: Lighter + ferro rod + waterproof matches (three ignition methods); tinder (petroleum jelly cotton balls or wax-based starter)
- [ ] Repair tools and knife: Fixed-blade knife or multi-tool, duct tape, paracord (50 ft minimum)
- [ ] Nutrition: 1-day extra food beyond planned
- [ ] Hydration: Water filter or purification tablets; extra capacity container
- [ ] Emergency shelter: Lightweight tarp or bivy + emergency mylar blanket
- [ ] Signaling: PLB or Garmin inReach; signal mirror; loud whistle
- [ ] Communication: Charged phone in dry bag; written emergency contact plan left with a trusted person

### Checklist: Improvised Debris Hut Build Sequence

- [ ] Select a site protected from wind, not in a drainage or low-lying cold air pool
- [ ] Gather ridgepole: a stout straight branch approximately 9-12 feet long
- [ ] Prop ridgepole at hip height on a fork, stump, or a bipod of crossed sticks
- [ ] Angle ribs (branches) against both sides of the ridgepole to form an A-frame skeleton
- [ ] Pile coarse debris (branches, sticks) over the ribs to form a lattice
- [ ] Heap dry leaves, pine needles, ferns, or grass over the lattice: minimum 2 feet deep all around (more is better)
- [ ] Create a door plug: a bundle of leaves stuffed into a garbage bag or packed into a mesh of sticks
- [ ] Lay at least 4-6 inches of dry debris on the floor for insulation from ground conduction
- [ ] Fill interior with armfuls of dry leaves to serve as a body-length sleeping bag
- [ ] Test fit: inside should be just wide enough to roll over; excess space is warmth lost
- [ ] Mark the location with a signal (bright material on top, visible from above)

### Checklist: Stop the Bleed: Hemorrhage Control Decision Sequence

- [ ] Ensure scene safety before approaching
- [ ] Call 911 (or direct someone to call) immediately if possible
- [ ] Identify the source of bleeding by exposing the wound
- [ ] LIMB bleeding, severe: Apply tourniquet 2-3 inches above wound (not over joint), tighten windlass until bleeding stops, write time on tourniquet or skin
- [ ] TORSO / JUNCTIONAL bleeding (groin, armpit, neck — tourniquet not applicable): Pack wound firmly with gauze pressing against bleeding source; apply sustained direct pressure minimum 3 minutes
- [ ] SURFACE / LACERATION: Apply firm direct pressure with cleanest material available; do not release for at least 10 minutes
- [ ] Maintain pressure until EMS arrives; do not remove a tourniquet once applied
- [ ] Monitor patient for shock: lay flat, elevate legs if no spinal injury suspected, maintain warmth
- [ ] Document time of tourniquet application and relay to EMS

### Checklist: Survival Signaling Priority Sequence

- [ ] Activate PLB or satellite communicator immediately if available and situation warrants
- [ ] Stay in a visible location: open ground, hilltop, clearing, lakeside
- [ ] Blow emergency whistle in groups of three at regular intervals
- [ ] Prepare a signal fire in a clearing (three fires in a triangle if resources allow)
- [ ] Construct ground-to-air symbol (V for help, X for medical) at least 10 feet tall in a clearing
- [ ] Use signal mirror whenever aircraft or sunlight is present: aim reflected beam toward aircraft
- [ ] Flash a light (three short, three long, three short = SOS) toward any visible lights at night
- [ ] Conserve voice; use whistle instead
- [ ] Do not move from your last known position unless movement is imperative for survival
