# Military reference (educational)

> **Disclaimer:** Educational / reference / preparedness material with links to public and open-source resources. Not operational instructions for violence or weapons. Verify against primary sources; train with qualified instructors. Follow all applicable laws.

Military reference covering U.S. Army rank structures, the Military Occupational Specialty (MOS) system, the public field-manual ecosystem (FM/ATP/TC/ADP), echelons of command from fire team to theater, the OODA loop decision-making framework, and the organization of fire teams and squads as published in open Army doctrine. All content is descriptive and educational, drawn from publicly available U.S. Army doctrine and open-source references. Primary authoritative source: Army Publishing Directorate at armypubs.army.mil.

## Rank Structure: Enlisted (E-1 to E-9)

U.S. Army enlisted soldiers are organized into three broad tiers across nine pay grades.

**Junior Enlisted (E-1 to E-4)**
- E-1 Private (PVT) — no insignia; initial entry / Basic Training
- E-2 Private Second Class (PV2) — one chevron point up
- E-3 Private First Class (PFC) — one chevron plus one rocker
- E-4 Specialist (SPC) or Corporal (CPL) — SPC wears eagle device (technical track); CPL wears two chevrons (leadership track, first NCO grade)

**Noncommissioned Officers (NCOs) — E-5 to E-6**
- E-5 Sergeant (SGT) — three chevrons; first full NCO rank; typically leads a fire team or vehicle crew
- E-6 Staff Sergeant (SSG) — three chevrons, one rocker; typically leads a rifle squad (8–10 soldiers)

**Senior Noncommissioned Officers (SNCOs) — E-7 to E-9**
- E-7 Sergeant First Class (SFC) — three chevrons, two rockers; typically platoon sergeant
- E-8 Master Sergeant (MSG) / First Sergeant (1SG) — three chevrons, three rockers; 1SG adds a diamond and is the senior NCO of a company-sized unit
- E-9 Sergeant Major (SGM) / Command Sergeant Major (CSM) / Sergeant Major of the Army (SMA) — three chevrons, three rockers, star; CSM is the senior NCO of a battalion or higher; SMA is the highest enlisted rank in the Army, senior advisor to the Chief of Staff

Note: E-4 CPL is the only junior-enlisted rank that holds NCO authority. The transition to SGT (E-5) is the most significant professional milestone for enlisted soldiers. Senior NCOs focus on organizational leadership, not just individual soldier supervision.

## Rank Structure: Warrant Officers (W-1 to W-5) and Commissioned Officers (O-1 to O-10)

**Warrant Officers — Technical Specialists**
Warrant officers are highly skilled technical and tactical experts, typically drawn from the enlisted force, who serve as officer-grade specialists rather than command generalists. They rank above senior NCOs but below commissioned officers.
- W-1 Warrant Officer 1 (WO1) — initial warrant grade; one black bar
- W-2 Chief Warrant Officer 2 (CW2) — fully functioning technical professional; working-level aviator, intelligence analyst, cyber specialist, etc.
- W-3 Chief Warrant Officer 3 (CW3) — senior technical authority; advises field-grade officers, mentors junior warrants
- W-4 Chief Warrant Officer 4 (CW4) — master-level expert; senior aviation standardization officer, senior intelligence technician
- W-5 Chief Warrant Officer 5 (CW5) — strategic-level technical advisor; rare; only created in 1992

Major warrant officer branches include aviation (most numerous), cyber, intelligence, human resources, ordnance, signal, and field artillery.

**Commissioned Officers — Command & Leadership**
Commissioned officers receive their authority from a presidential commission. Three tiers:

Junior Officers (Company Grade):
- O-1 Second Lieutenant (2LT) — one gold bar; typically platoon leader (~16–44 soldiers)
- O-2 First Lieutenant (1LT) — one silver bar
- O-3 Captain (CPT) — two silver bars; commands a company (~80–200 soldiers); first "command" role

Field Grade Officers:
- O-4 Major (MAJ) — gold oak leaf; staff role or battalion executive officer
- O-5 Lieutenant Colonel (LTC) — silver oak leaf; commands a battalion (~300–1,000 soldiers)
- O-6 Colonel (COL) — silver eagle; commands a brigade (~3,000–5,000 soldiers)

General Officers:
- O-7 Brigadier General (BG) — one star
- O-8 Major General (MG) — two stars; division command (~10,000–15,000 soldiers)
- O-9 Lieutenant General (LTG) — three stars; corps command
- O-10 General (GEN) — four stars; Army component or unified command
- General of the Army (five stars) — wartime only; not currently active

The Army Chief of Staff is a four-star general and the senior military officer of the Army. The Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff (CJCS) is the senior uniformed officer of the entire U.S. military.

## Military Occupational Specialty (MOS) System

The MOS system is the Army's job classification framework. Each soldier is assigned a specific MOS that defines their primary duty, training pipeline, and career path.

**MOS Code Format**
A standard Army enlisted MOS code is alphanumeric: two digits identifying the Career Management Field (CMF) + one letter identifying the specific specialty. Example: 11B = CMF 11 (Infantry) + B (Infantryman). Officer AOCs (Area of Concentration) follow a similar pattern.

**Career Management Fields (CMFs) — Selected Examples**
- CMF 11 — Infantry (11B Infantryman, 11C Indirect Fire Infantryman/Mortarman)
- CMF 12 — Combat Engineering (12B Combat Engineer, 12C Bridge Crewmember)
- CMF 13 — Field Artillery (13B Cannon Crewmember, 13F Fire Support Specialist)
- CMF 15 — Aviation (15T UH-60 Helicopter Repairer, 15W Unmanned Aircraft Systems Operator)
- CMF 25 — Signal / Communications (25U Signal Support Systems Specialist)
- CMF 35 — Military Intelligence (35F Intelligence Analyst, 35N Signals Intelligence Analyst)
- CMF 68 — Medical (68W Combat Medic Specialist — the primary enlisted medic MOS)
- CMF 91 — Mechanical Maintenance (91B Wheeled Vehicle Mechanic)
- CMF 92 — Supply and Logistics (92A Automated Logistical Specialist, 92F Petroleum Supply Specialist)

**Scope**
Approximately 190 enlisted MOS designations exist across the Army. Many have direct civilian equivalents (engineering, IT, healthcare, logistics). The MOS code appears on the soldier's personnel file and determines assignment eligibility, promotion competition category, and re-enlistment bonuses.

**Additional Skill Identifiers (ASIs) and Special Qualifications Identifiers (SQIs)**
ASIs and SQIs are appended to the MOS to denote special skills such as airborne qualification (P), Ranger tab (V), or special forces (S). Example: 11B2P = Infantryman, pay grade E-4/E-5, airborne qualified.

**MOSQ (MOS Qualified)**
A soldier becomes MOSQ after completing the relevant Advanced Individual Training (AIT) or One Station Unit Training (OSUT) course at the appropriate Army school (e.g., Infantry OSUT at Fort Moore; 68W AIT at Fort Sam Houston).

## Echelons of Command: Fire Team to Theater

U.S. Army doctrine organizes forces into standardized echelons. Each echelon has a doctrinal size range, a designated commander rank, and a specific tactical or operational role.

**Tactical Echelons (ground up)**

1. Fire Team (4 soldiers) — Led by a SGT (E-5); smallest doctrinal maneuver element. Standard infantry fire team: Team Leader, automatic rifleman (M249 SAW or M27 IAR), grenadier (M203/M320), and rifleman. Two fire teams (Alpha + Bravo) form a squad.

2. Squad (8–10 soldiers) — Led by a SSG (E-6); two fire teams plus the squad leader. The squad is the Army's fundamental tactical unit. Squad leader controls both teams via radio and direct communication.

3. Section (varies) — Two or more squads, or a weapons crew; used in armor, artillery, and support contexts. Led by a SSG or SFC.

4. Platoon (16–44 soldiers) — Led by a 2LT/1LT with a SFC as platoon sergeant. Typically 3–4 squads. The platoon sergeant is second in command and the senior NCO voice.

5. Company / Battery / Troop (80–200 soldiers) — Led by a CPT with a 1SG as senior NCO. First unit with its own administrative records, supply accounts, and duty rosters. "Battery" = artillery; "Troop" = cavalry.

6. Battalion / Squadron (~300–1,000 soldiers) — Led by a LTC with a CSM as senior NCO. 3–5 companies. "Squadron" = cavalry. First unit with full staff (S1–S6 staff sections).

7. Brigade / Regiment / Group (~3,000–5,000 soldiers) — Led by a COL with a CSM. 3–5 battalions. The Brigade Combat Team (BCT) is the Army's primary deployable combined-arms maneuver unit. Three BCT types: Infantry (IBCT), Armored (ABCT), and Stryker (SBCT). Note: a Mobile BCT (MBCT) type was added as of 2024 reorganization discussions.

8. Division (~10,000–15,000 soldiers) — Led by a MG with a CSM. 3 BCTs plus division support and fires brigades. The division is the primary operational headquarters for land combat.

9. Corps (~20,000–45,000 soldiers) — Led by a LTG. Multiple divisions. Synchronizes joint fires, logistics, and operations across a large geographic area.

10. Field Army (50,000–100,000+ soldiers) — Led by a GEN. Multiple corps. Operational to strategic level.

11. Theater Army — Led by a GEN. Army Service Component Command assigned to a Combatant Commander (e.g., USAREUR-AF in Europe). Provides command and control, sustainment, and land force integration across a geographic combatant command.

**Key doctrinal principle:** Each echelon receives its mission from one level up and assigns tasks to units two levels down ("two levels down" planning).

## The OODA Loop: Military Decision-Making Framework

The OODA loop is a decision-making model developed by U.S. Air Force Colonel John Boyd in the early 1970s, derived from his analysis of air-to-air combat effectiveness during the Korean War. Boyd observed that American F-86 pilots achieved a kill ratio of approximately 10:1 over MiG-15 pilots despite the MiG's technical advantages. He attributed this to pilot decision speed and cockpit visibility — the ability to cycle through decisions faster.

**The Four Stages**

1. Observe — Gather raw information from the environment: sensor data, reconnaissance reports, intelligence feeds, direct observation. The quality of observation determines downstream quality of the entire loop.

2. Orient — The most critical and most complex stage. Filter and interpret observations through mental models shaped by cultural traditions, prior experience, analysis/synthesis processes, and new information simultaneously. Orientation is the lens through which all observation is interpreted. Boyd considered this the "schwerpunkt" (center of gravity) of the model.

3. Decide — Select a course of action from the options generated by orientation. In fast-moving tactical environments, this stage may collapse into near-automatic response for well-trained soldiers.

4. Act — Execute the decision. The action re-enters the environment and generates new observations, restarting the loop.

**Key Insight: Tempo and Agility**
Boyd's central argument: the combatant who can cycle through the OODA loop faster than an opponent creates increasing uncertainty and confusion in the opponent's decision process — eventually collapsing the opponent's ability to act coherently. "Getting inside the adversary's OODA loop" means acting so rapidly and unpredictably that the opponent's orientation and decide phases become irrelevant.

**Application in Army Doctrine**
The OODA loop is referenced throughout Army doctrine as a conceptual framework for understanding tempo in combat. It underpins concepts such as mission command (decentralized decision authority to subordinates), reconnaissance operations (accelerate Observe), and combined arms (complicate the adversary's Orient phase by presenting multiple simultaneous threats). ADP 6-0 (Mission Command) and FM 3-0 (Operations) both address the importance of decision tempo.

**Beyond Military Use**
Boyd's framework has been adopted in business strategy, emergency management, law enforcement, and competitive sports. The OODA loop is publicly documented in Boyd's briefing "Patterns of Conflict" (declassified, available from various academic sources).

## The Public Field-Manual Ecosystem: FM, ATP, TC, ADP

The U.S. Army's doctrine publication system is publicly available at the Army Publishing Directorate (APD): armypubs.army.mil. Most unclassified publications are free PDF downloads.

**Publication Type Hierarchy**

1. Army Doctrine Publications (ADP) — Capstone, short, foundational. Cover the most essential principles. Examples:
   - ADP 1: The Army (Army identity and ethos)
   - ADP 3-0: Operations (overarching operations doctrine)
   - ADP 6-22: Army Leadership and the Profession

2. Army Doctrine Reference Publications (ADRP) — Expand on ADP content with more detailed explanation. (Note: ADRPs were being consolidated back into FMs in the early 2020s revision cycle.)

3. Field Manuals (FM) — Comprehensive tactical and operational reference manuals. Most detailed. Examples:
   - FM 3-0: Operations (March 2025 edition; describes large-scale combat operations and multidomain operations)
   - FM 3-21.8 / ATP 3-21.8: The Infantry Rifle Platoon and Squad
   - FM 6-22: Developing Leaders
   - FM 2-0: Intelligence
   - FM 3-60: Army Targeting

4. Army Techniques Publications (ATP) — Tactics-focused; describe how to accomplish specific tasks. Subordinate to FMs. Examples:
   - ATP 3-21.8: Infantry Platoon and Squad (key tactical reference)
   - ATP 3-34.81: Engineer Reconnaissance
   - ATP 3-90.1: Armor and Mechanized Infantry Company Team

5. Training Circulars (TC) — Training-specific guidance. Most famous example:
   - TC 3-21.76: Ranger Handbook (April 2017 edition) — the canonical field reference for small-unit tactics, navigation, survival, and patrol operations. Approved for public release with unlimited distribution. Available directly from APD: armypubs.army.mil/epubs/DR_pubs/DR_a/ARN3039-TC_3-21.76-000-WEB-1.pdf

**How to Access**
- Direct URL: https://armypubs.army.mil — search by number or keyword
- Product maps for each type: armypubs.army.mil/ProductMaps/PubForm/FM.aspx (Field Manuals), /ADP.aspx, /ATP.aspx, /TC.aspx
- Federation of American Scientists (FAS) mirrors many older publications: irp.fas.org/doddir/army/
- Internet Archive hosts scanned editions of older FMs: archive.org

**Distribution Restriction Codes**
Most tactical publications are "Distribution Statement A" (approved for public release, unlimited distribution). Some are restricted to U.S. military and government contractors. The distribution statement appears on the cover page of every publication. Never redistribute a restricted manual to unauthorized parties.

## Infantry Fire Team and Squad: Published Doctrine

The primary doctrinal source for fire team and squad organization and tactics is ATP 3-21.8 (Infantry Platoon and Squad), available at armypubs.army.mil, and the Ranger Handbook TC 3-21.76 for field reference.

**Standard Rifle Squad Composition (9 soldiers, two fire teams)**

Squad Leader (SSG / E-6) — commands both fire teams; positioned to control the battle

Alpha Fire Team (4 soldiers, led by SGT E-5):
- Team Leader (SGT) — M4 carbine, responsible for team movement and fire control
- Automatic Rifleman — M249 SAW or M27 IAR; primary base of fire
- Grenadier — M4 with M320 grenade launcher (40mm); indirect suppression
- Rifleman — M4 carbine; often carries AT4 or additional ammunition

Bravo Fire Team (4 soldiers, same structure)

**Fire Team Battle Drills**
Doctrinal battle drills are immediate, trained responses executed without deliberate planning. Published drills include: react to contact, break contact, react to near and far ambush, knock out a bunker, enter and clear a room. All are detailed in TC 3-21.76 and ATP 3-21.8.

**Weapons Systems Integration**
The fire team is designed around the "base of fire / maneuver" concept: one element suppresses while another moves. The automatic rifleman provides the base of fire; the rifleman and grenadier maneuver under that suppression.

**Leadership Responsibilities**
The team leader (SGT) is responsible for the positioning, fire control, movement, and welfare of all four team members. The squad leader (SSG) synchronizes both teams, communicates with the platoon leader, and executes the platoon's scheme of maneuver at squad level. The NCO creed governs these responsibilities at all levels.

**Formations**
Doctrinal squad and team formations include: file (movement in dense terrain, stealth), column (road movement), line (assault, maximum frontal fire), wedge (all-around security, flexible), echelon left/right (flank protection). All described in ATP 3-21.8 and TC 3-21.76.

## Key Doctrinal Concepts and Principles

Several overarching concepts appear repeatedly across Army doctrine and are essential to understanding the system.

**Mission Command (ADP 6-0)**
The Army's command philosophy. Commanders exercise authority through the mission-type order: they communicate intent two levels down ("commander's intent"), give subordinates maximum freedom of action within that intent, and expect decentralized execution. This is the doctrinal counter to rigid top-down control and is directly tied to OODA-loop speed.

**Unified Land Operations / Multidomain Operations (FM 3-0)**
The current capstone operational concept. The Army operates across all domains — land, air, sea, space, cyberspace, and the information environment — simultaneously. Large-scale combat operations (LSCO) against a peer or near-peer adversary is the primary planning scenario as of FM 3-0 (2022/2025).

**Combined Arms (doctrine-wide)**
The synchronized application of two or more arms (infantry, armor, artillery, aviation, engineers, air defense, information) to achieve an effect that no single arm can achieve alone. Combined arms is the foundational organizing principle from platoon to theater.

**MDMP: Military Decision-Making Process (FM 6-0)**
The Army's formal staff planning process: Receipt of Mission → Mission Analysis → Course of Action (COA) Development → COA Analysis (wargame) → COA Comparison → COA Approval → Orders Production. MDMP produces the Operations Order (OPORD).

**OPORD Five-Paragraph Format (SMEAC)**
1. Situation (enemy/friendly/attachments)
2. Mission (who, what, when, where, why — the task and purpose)
3. Execution (commander's intent, concept of operations, tasks to subordinates, coordinating instructions)
4. Service and Support (logistics, medical, transportation)
5. Command and Signal (chain of command, communication plan, signals)
This five-paragraph format is used from squad leader to theater commander. TC 3-21.76 (Ranger Handbook) contains a field-condensed version.

**Troop Leading Procedures (TLP)**
The small-unit leader's version of MDMP, condensed for time-constrained tactical environments: Receive the Mission → Issue a Warning Order → Make a Tentative Plan → Initiate Movement → Conduct Reconnaissance → Complete the Plan → Issue the Order → Supervise. The TLP is detailed in TC 3-21.76 and ATP 3-21.8.

**IPB: Intelligence Preparation of the Battlefield (FM 2-01.3)**
The analytical process for understanding how terrain, weather, and the enemy affect friendly operations. Four steps: Define the environment → Describe the environment's effects → Evaluate the threat → Determine threat courses of action.

### Checklist: Locating a Public Army Doctrine Manual

- [ ] Go to armypubs.army.mil
- [ ] Use the search bar or navigate to ProductMaps (FM, ATP, TC, ADP tabs)
- [ ] Enter the publication number (e.g., TC 3-21.76, FM 3-0, ADP 6-22)
- [ ] Check the Distribution Statement on the cover: Statement A = unlimited public release
- [ ] Download the PDF directly from the APD link (free, no registration required)
- [ ] For older or archived manuals, check archive.org or irp.fas.org/doddir/army/
- [ ] Verify the publication date — doctrine is revised; always confirm you have the current edition

### Checklist: Five-Paragraph Operations Order (SMEAC) Checklist

- [ ] 1. SITUATION: Enemy forces (composition, disposition, capabilities), friendly forces (higher unit mission, adjacent units), attachments/detachments
- [ ] 2. MISSION: Who, What, When, Where, Why — task and purpose stated in one sentence
- [ ] 3. EXECUTION: Commander's intent, concept of operations, tasks to each subordinate unit, coordinating instructions (actions on contact, ROE, consolidation plan)
- [ ] 4. SERVICE AND SUPPORT (also called Administration and Logistics): Sustainment plan, medical evacuation, resupply, prisoner handling
- [ ] 5. COMMAND AND SIGNAL: Chain of command, location of commander, communication plan (frequencies, call signs, challenge/password)
- [ ] Issue Warning Order as soon as mission is received — do not wait for complete plan
- [ ] Use terrain model or map overlay to brief; confirm all subordinates understand commander's intent

### Checklist: Troop Leading Procedures (TLP) — Small-Unit Leader Sequence

- [ ] 1. Receive the Mission — analyze higher unit order
- [ ] 2. Issue a Warning Order — alert subordinates immediately so they can begin preparation
- [ ] 3. Make a Tentative Plan — apply METT-TC (Mission, Enemy, Terrain & weather, Troops available, Time, Civilians)
- [ ] 4. Initiate Movement — if required; don't wait for complete plan
- [ ] 5. Conduct Reconnaissance — leader recon of objective and routes if time permits
- [ ] 6. Complete the Plan — refine based on recon results
- [ ] 7. Issue the Order — full OPORD to all subordinates
- [ ] 8. Supervise — rehearsals, precombat checks, precombat inspections (PCIs)

### Checklist: METT-TC Mission Analysis Factors

- [ ] M — Mission: What is the task and purpose?
- [ ] E — Enemy: Composition, disposition, strength, capabilities, most likely/dangerous course of action
- [ ] T — Terrain and Weather: Key terrain, observation and fields of fire, avenues of approach, cover and concealment, obstacles; weather effects
- [ ] T — Troops available: What friendly forces are available; their training, readiness, and attachments
- [ ] T — Time: How much time is available for planning, movement, execution, and consolidation?
- [ ] C — Civilians: Civilian presence on the battlefield; legal constraints; information environment
