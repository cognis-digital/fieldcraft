# Rucking & load-bearing

> **Disclaimer:** Educational / reference / preparedness material with links to public and open-source resources. Not operational instructions for violence or weapons. Verify against primary sources; train with qualified instructors. Follow all applicable laws.

Rucking — walking or marching with a weighted pack — is the foundational physical activity of infantry soldiering and has become a widely adopted civilian fitness and preparedness practice. U.S. Army doctrine codifies three load categories (fighting, approach march, and sustainment), each with defined weight thresholds and tactical purposes. The governing publication is ATP 3-21.18 (Foot Marches), most recently revised in 2022 and again in July 2025. For civilians, the 1/3-bodyweight upper limit drawn from FM 21-18 is widely cited as a ceiling for conditioned individuals; beginners should start at 10% of bodyweight. Proper gear selection, progressive overload, load distribution, and systematic foot care are the core pillars that separate productive rucking from chronic injury. This entry covers Army doctrine weight standards, multi-branch benchmarks, load packing principles, foot care, common injuries, progressive training, and hydration/nutrition guidance.

## Army Load Doctrine: Three Load Categories

U.S. Army doctrine (ATP 3-21.18, formerly FM 21-18) defines three primary soldier load categories:

**Fighting Load** — Only the essential items a soldier needs to maneuver, close with, and destroy enemy forces once in direct contact. Doctrine repeatedly recommends this not exceed approximately 48 lbs. It includes weapon, ammunition, body armor, water, individual first aid kit (IFAK), and mission-critical communications gear. The fighting load is what the soldier retains after dropping any additional pack at the objective rally point (ORP).

**Approach March Load** — The fighting load plus a rucksack containing additional water, ammunition, food, and mission-sustaining supplies carried during movement to the objective. Dropped at the ORP before direct action. Doctrine cites approximately 72 lbs as the upper limit. ATP 3-21.18 notes that loads exceeding 55–60 lbs measurably degrade speed, maneuverability, and combat performance.

**Sustainment Load** — Equipment needed for extended overnight or multi-day operations: sleeping bag, shelter, extra clothing, rations, and additional ammunition. Can include squad duffel bags. No single hard weight ceiling is specified in doctrine; rather, sustainment loads are broken out into vehicle transport or logistics packages whenever possible to minimize the burden on dismounted soldiers.

The commander bears doctrinal responsibility for tailoring all three loads to the mission: "Understanding that ounces can add up to pounds and pounds impact performance, commanders ensure the equipment Soldiers carry during the mission is essential and must be carried" (ATP 3-21.18). This principle — load management as a leadership function, not just an individual one — is a central theme of current Army doctrine. Excessive loads degrade physical readiness, combat performance, and can cause mission failure.

Note: FM 7-21.13, The Soldier's Guide (updated as TC 7-21.13, September 2025), is the companion pocket reference that addresses individual soldier preparation including load-bearing fundamentals.

## 1/3 Bodyweight Guideline and Civilian Load Scaling

The widely cited "one-third of bodyweight" rule originates in Army field manual guidance (FM 21-18 lineage) as the upper limit for a conditioned soldier's approach march load. At 180 lbs bodyweight, that equates to 60 lbs — closely matching the ~72-lb approach march ceiling when body armor and other worn gear are included.

For untrained civilians and beginners, exercise physiologists and military fitness coaches strongly recommend a much more conservative entry point:

- **Beginners:** Start at 10% of bodyweight (18 lbs for a 180-lb person). Walk 2–3 miles, 2–3 times per week on flat ground.
- **Intermediate:** Progress to 15–20% of bodyweight over 6–8 weeks as conditioning builds.
- **Advanced/Military prep:** 20–30% of bodyweight, with emphasis on technique and terrain variety.
- **Hard ceiling (conditioned):** 1/3 bodyweight per doctrine; loads above this level require specific mission justification and recovery planning.

Research cited by human performance organizations such as O2X indicates that loads of 17% bodyweight already produce measurable changes in spinal alignment, and that increases beyond 30% sharply elevate injury risk for non-acclimatized individuals.

The single most important form indicator: if your posture changes — forward lean increases, shoulders roll, stride shortens abnormally — the load is too heavy for that training session. Reduce weight before compensatory movement patterns cause overuse injury.

## Military Ruck March Standards by Branch and Program

The standard Army tactical foot march benchmark is **12 miles in under 3 hours (15 min/mile pace) with a 35-lb pack**. This is the minimum standard tested across most conventional Army units and is the baseline for Air Assault school qualification.

Note: As of June 1, 2025, the Army Fitness Test (AFT) replaced the Army Combat Fitness Test (ACFT) as the official test of record. Neither the ACFT nor the AFT includes a ruck march event as a scored component; ruck proficiency is assessed separately at the unit level and in specialty-school selection pipelines.

**Standards by program (approximate; verify against current school regulations):**

| Program | Distance | Weight | Time Limit |
|---|---|---|---|
| Army standard (conventional) | 12 miles | 35 lbs pack | 3 hours |
| Air Assault (6-mile) | 6 miles | 35 lbs | 90 min |
| Air Assault (12-mile) | 12 miles | 35 lbs | 3 hours |
| RASP (Ranger Regt selection) | 12 miles | 45+ lbs | 3 hours |
| RASP (short events) | 4–6 miles | 45 lbs | 60–90 min |
| Ranger School training | 12–15 miles | 45–55 lbs | ~15 min/mile |
| SFAS (individual march) | 12 miles | 45 lbs | 3 hours |
| SFAS (Long Range Individual Movement / "the Trek") | up to 32 miles | 40–60 lbs | timed |
| Marine Corps (20-mile forced march) | 20 miles | 45 lbs | 4 hours |
| Marine Corps typical combat load | varies | 74–101 lbs total | — |

Civilians preparing for events such as GORUCK Challenges typically train to the 12-mile/3-hour/35-lb standard as a baseline. Special Forces prep coaches recommend achieving 12 miles in 2:15–2:30 with 50 lbs before attending SFAS.

## Load Distribution and Pack Packing Principles

How weight is positioned in the pack matters as much as total weight. Incorrect distribution increases spinal loading, shifts the body's center of gravity rearward, causes compensatory forward lean, and accelerates fatigue in the lower back and knees.

**Packing hierarchy (top to bottom, front to back):**

1. **Heaviest items highest and closest to the back** — sleeping bag plates, water bladders, ammunition, food. This keeps the load's center of mass near the upper thoracic spine where the body carries load most efficiently.
2. **Medium-weight items in the middle tier** — clothing, cook gear, secondary water.
3. **Lightest items at the bottom and on the outside** — rain gear, softgoods, sleeping pad.
4. **Frequent-access items in top lid or hip-belt pockets** — snacks, map, gloves, first aid kit.

**Hip belt is critical.** Properly fitted, the hip belt transfers 60–80% of pack weight from the shoulders to the hips and pelvis, which are biomechanically designed to carry load. Wearing a rucksack with the hip belt unfastened or improperly positioned concentrates all stress on the shoulders and cervical spine — a common cause of neck, trap, and rotator cuff injuries on long marches.

**Ruck fit checks:**
- Shoulder straps should touch the shoulders without digging; there should be no gap between straps and the tops of the shoulders.
- The hip belt should sit on top of the iliac crest, not on the waist.
- The load lifter straps (if present) should make a 45-degree angle to the top of the pack.
- Pack should not sag or bounce. Excessive movement creates lateral forces that fatigue stabilizer muscles and stress the knees.

Research notes that a rucksack's center of mass positioned above the hips reduces the forward trunk lean required for balance, lowering the metabolic cost of the march and reducing compressive loading on lumbar vertebrae.

## Foot Care and Blister Prevention

Blisters are the leading cause of attrition on long foot marches. They result from heat, moisture, and friction — all of which are manageable with proper preparation.

**Boot selection and fit:**
- Boots should fit snug in the heel with toe wiggle room. A thumb's width between the longest toe and the boot toe box is the standard check.
- New boots must be broken in gradually (walk 2–3 miles per session, progressively increasing) before any long ruck. Do not attempt a 12-mile march in boots worn fewer than 40–50 miles.
- Leather and full-grain uppers provide durability; modern textile/mesh boots dry faster but offer less lateral support under load.

**Sock system (two-layer method, Joint Services-recommended):**
1. **Inner liner:** Thin synthetic-blend (polyester/nylon) or merino wool sock worn next to skin. Wicks moisture outward. Some soldiers use knee-high nylon stockings as the inner liner — this allows slipping/friction to occur between nylon and sock rather than between sock and skin.
2. **Outer boot sock:** Thicker wool or wool-blend sock worn turned inside-out so seams face away from skin. The friction plane shifts to the sock-on-sock interface rather than sock-on-skin.
- Avoid 100% cotton: it retains moisture, stays wet, and dramatically accelerates maceration and friction injury.

**Pre-ruck skin preparation:**
- Trim toenails straight across, short enough not to press on boot toe box.
- File down callus edges and any rough nail edges — these snag socks and create focal-pressure blisters.
- Apply Body Glide, petroleum jelly, or anti-chafe balm to known hotspots (heels, ball of foot, between toes) before the march.
- Gold Bond powder or similar in socks reduces moisture during the march.
- Pre-tape known hotspots with kinesiology tape or moleskin.

**During the march:**
- Change into dry socks at resupply halts or at mile 6 on a 12-mile march.
- At the first sensation of a hotspot, stop and address it. A 90-second sock adjustment prevents a blister that would cost 30 minutes at the end.

**Field blister treatment (U.S. Army guidance):**
- Small intact blisters: Do not pop. Apply a moleskin doughnut (cut a hole in the center over the blister) to offload pressure, then cover with a second moleskin layer.
- Large, painful blisters that prevent walking: Sterilize a pin with alcohol. Pierce at the blister's edge (not the center). Drain gently, leaving the roof intact — the skin roof is the best biological dressing available in the field. Apply antibiotic ointment and cover with clean dressing and moleskin.
- Seek medical attention for any blister showing signs of infection (redness spreading from the margin, warmth, pus, red streaks), or for blisters that prevent weight-bearing.

## Progressive Training and Injury Prevention

The 10 most common rucking injuries are: blisters, metatarsalgia, plantar fasciitis, stress fractures, shin splints, lower back pain, hip bursitis, patellofemoral pain syndrome (runner's knee), Achilles tendinitis, and shoulder impingement. All are overuse injuries, and nearly all are preventable with progressive programming.

**Core progressive overload rule:** Increase only ONE variable per week — either distance, weight, or pace. Never increase any variable by more than 10–20% in a single week.

**Beginner 6-week baseline structure:**
- Weeks 1–2: 30–45 min rucks, flat terrain, 10–15% bodyweight, 2x per week
- Weeks 3–4: 45–60 min rucks, add one hill or stair segment, 15–20% bodyweight, 3x per week
- Weeks 5–6: 60–90 min rucks, varied terrain, 20–25% bodyweight, 3x per week
- Deload every 4 weeks: reduce distance and weight by 30–40% for one week

**Strength training complements rucking:** Target the posterior chain (deadlifts, RDLs), core (planks, loaded carries), lower body (squats, lunges, step-ups), and glute activation (hip thrusts). Weak glutes and underdeveloped posterior chain are the primary contributors to lower back pain and knee pain under load.

**Technique under load:**
- Maintain upright posture with shoulders back and core engaged. Forward lean should be minimal and deliberate on incline.
- Take shorter, quicker steps rather than long heel-strike strides. Aim for mid-foot contact to distribute ground reaction forces.
- On downhill: shorten stride, slightly bend knees, lean back slightly to prevent quad overload and patellofemoral stress.

**Frequency and recovery:**
- Beginners: 2–3 rucks per week on non-consecutive days.
- Intermediate/advanced: up to 4 rucks per week maximum; 2–3 complete rest days per week.
- Daily rucking is contraindicated for most individuals due to insufficient tendon and bone recovery time.
- Shin splints warning: dull aching pain along the medial tibia after or during rucking. Reduce volume by 30–50%, switch to softer surfaces, and allow 4–6 weeks before returning to full load. Untreated, shin splints progress to tibial stress fractures.

**Red flags requiring medical evaluation:** Sharp or localized bone pain (possible stress fracture); pain that worsens during activity (rather than warms up and improves); swelling or discoloration around a joint; inability to bear weight; knee instability; tingling or numbness in extremities.

## Hydration and Nutrition for Ruck Marches

Hydration failure is the fastest route to degraded performance and heat injury on long foot marches. The Army and military performance organizations (including Ft. Drum's Nutrition for Ruck Marches guidance) emphasize that hydration begins the day before the event.

**Water intake guidelines:**
- Pre-ruck: Drink 16–20 oz of water 2 hours before stepping off; another 8 oz 30 minutes before.
- During: Approximately 16–24 oz (0.5–0.75L) per hour in moderate conditions; up to 1L/hour in heat. Do not wait for thirst — thirst signals lag behind actual dehydration.
- Avoid over-hydration with plain water on very long marches (hyponatremia risk): add electrolytes (sodium, potassium, magnesium) every 1–2 hours.
- Post-ruck: Rehydrate to replace sweat losses; urine should be pale yellow within 2 hours of finishing.

**Electrolytes:** Critical on events exceeding 2 hours. Sodium loss through sweat is the primary electrolyte concern. Salt tablets, sports drinks, or electrolyte packets (Nuun, LMNT, etc.) prevent cramping and cognitive degradation that plain water consumption alone cannot address.

**Nutrition:**
- Pre-ruck meal (2–3 hours before): carbohydrate-dominant with moderate protein — oatmeal, eggs, rice, toast. Avoid high-fat or high-fiber foods that slow gastric emptying.
- During (events over 90 minutes): 30–60g of carbohydrate per hour. Energy bars, dried fruit, nuts, crackers, gels, or MRE components all work.
- Post-ruck (within 30–60 minutes): protein + carbohydrate combination to begin glycogen resynthesis and muscle repair. 20–30g protein recommended.
- For multi-day operations, adequate caloric intake is mission-critical: sustained caloric deficit combined with high physical output accelerates muscle catabolism, impairs immune function, and raises injury risk.

**Pack organization for nutrition:** Keep snacks and water/electrolyte sources in accessible pockets — hip belt pockets, top-lid pockets — to allow feeding without stopping and unpacking the main ruck.

## Gear Essentials: Packs, Boots, and Load Plates

The minimal gear required to ruck is any backpack and any heavy object. However, purpose-designed equipment meaningfully improves performance, comfort, and injury prevention at serious training loads.

**Pack selection:**
- A 20–40L pack is suitable for day rucks and military-standard events.
- Internal frame packs with padded hip belts and load lifter straps distribute weight most efficiently.
- MOLLE-compatible packs (Alice, USGI ILBE, FILBE, GORUCK GR series) are the standard for military preparation and GORUCK events.
- Pack should hold its shape under load. Soft bags without frames allow weight to shift and sag — avoid for loads above 20 lbs.

**Weight loading options:**
- **Ruck plates:** Flat steel or cast-iron plates sized to fit against the pack's back panel. Preferred because they sit close to the body's center of mass and don't shift. GORUCK, Rogue, and 5.11 all manufacture purpose-built ruck plates.
- **Sandbags:** Flexible, cheap, and realistic. Drawback: weight shifts during movement, increasing energy expenditure and hip stabilization demand.
- **Water, food, books, canned goods:** Adequate for beginners starting at 10–15 lbs.

**Boots vs. trail runners:**
- Above-ankle boots provide lateral stability and blister protection for sustained heavy loads (40+ lbs) and rough terrain. Essential for military standards.
- Trail runners reduce ankle fatigue on lighter loads and flat-to-moderate terrain. Popular among ultramarathon-style ruckers.
- Either requires being fully broken in before any event exceeding 6 miles.

**Essential accessories:**
- Moisture-wicking liner socks + wool/synthetic boot socks (two-layer system)
- Moleskin, Leukotape, or kinesiology tape
- Foot lubricant (Body Glide or petroleum jelly)
- Trekking poles: optional but significantly reduce quad/knee loading on downhill segments with heavy loads; used routinely in SFAS-prep training
- Hydration bladder (2–3L Camelbak or equivalent) for hands-free drinking

### Checklist: Pre-Ruck Preparation Checklist

- [ ] Weigh pack with all contents before stepping off — know your exact load
- [ ] Confirm load is 10% BW (beginner) or within your trained range
- [ ] Heaviest items positioned high and against the back panel
- [ ] Hip belt seated on iliac crest, not the waist; tighten until load transfers to hips
- [ ] Shoulder straps adjusted so no gap between strap and shoulder top
- [ ] Load lifter straps at approximately 45-degree angle (if present)
- [ ] Pack does not sag or bounce when walking
- [ ] Toenails trimmed straight and short
- [ ] Calluses and rough nail edges filed smooth
- [ ] Anti-chafe balm or petroleum jelly applied to heels, ball of foot, between toes
- [ ] Liner sock (synthetic) worn first; boot sock turned inside-out over it
- [ ] Pre-tape known hotspot areas with moleskin or Leukotape
- [ ] Gold Bond or moisture-absorbing powder in socks (optional)
- [ ] Boots are fully broken in (40+ miles prior wear)
- [ ] Hydration bladder filled (2–3L minimum); electrolyte tabs or packets packed
- [ ] Pre-march meal eaten 2–3 hours prior (carbs + protein, low fat/fiber)
- [ ] 16–20 oz water consumed 2 hours before; 8 oz within 30 min of start
- [ ] Snacks accessible in hip-belt or top-lid pockets (not buried in main compartment)
- [ ] Spare dry socks in accessible pocket or top lid
- [ ] Moleskin, pins, antibiotic ointment, and bandages in field first aid kit
- [ ] Weather and terrain appropriate footwear selected

### Checklist: During the Ruck — Form and Pacing Checks

- [ ] Posture: shoulders back, core engaged, forward gaze (not feet)
- [ ] Stride: shorter and quicker rather than long heel-strike steps
- [ ] Foot contact: aim for mid-foot landing, not heel strike
- [ ] On hills: slightly increase lean and shorten stride; do not bounce
- [ ] On downhill: shorten stride, mild knee bend, slow pace to protect quads and knees
- [ ] Drink 16–24 oz water per hour; do not wait for thirst
- [ ] Add electrolytes every 1–2 hours on events over 90 minutes
- [ ] Consume 30–60g carbohydrate per hour on events over 90 minutes
- [ ] At first hotspot sensation on foot: stop, remove boot, re-sock or tape immediately
- [ ] Change into dry socks at halts on marches over 6 miles
- [ ] Check hip belt and shoulder strap adjustment every 3–4 miles — straps loosen with sweat
- [ ] Note any sharp bone pain, joint swelling, or numbness — these are stop signals

### Checklist: Progressive Training Safety Rules

- [ ] Increase only ONE variable per week: distance, weight, or pace — never all three
- [ ] Maximum 10–20% increase per week in any single variable
- [ ] Ruck 2–3 days per week (beginner); no more than 4 days/week (advanced)
- [ ] Minimum 1 full rest day between ruck sessions
- [ ] Deload every 4th week: reduce distance and weight 30–40%
- [ ] Add strength training 2–3x per week: focus on posterior chain, core, glutes
- [ ] Do not ruck on consecutive days until conditioned (8+ weeks consistent training)
- [ ] If shin pain develops: reduce volume 30–50%, move to soft surfaces, no increase for 2 weeks
- [ ] If knee pain develops: stop downhill segments, reduce weight, evaluate quad/hamstring strength
- [ ] If lower back pain develops: check pack fit, reduce weight, add core stability work
- [ ] Seek medical evaluation for: localized sharp bone pain, swelling, inability to weight-bear, worsening pain during activity

### Checklist: Field Blister Treatment Protocol

- [ ] Small intact blister: do NOT pop
- [ ] Cut a doughnut hole in moleskin centered over blister to offload pressure
- [ ] Apply doughnut over blister; cover with second moleskin or bandage layer
- [ ] Large painful blister preventing walking: sterilize needle/pin with alcohol flame or alcohol wipe
- [ ] Pierce blister at edge (not center)
- [ ] Drain fluid gently by pressing from sides — leave skin roof intact
- [ ] Apply antibiotic ointment (bacitracin, triple antibiotic) to roof and surrounding skin
- [ ] Cover with non-stick gauze or bandage; layer moleskin doughnut over top
- [ ] Change dressing at next halt; keep clean and dry
- [ ] Seek sick call / medical if: spreading redness, warmth, pus, red streaks from site, fever, or failure to improve in 24h
