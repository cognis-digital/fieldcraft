# Field hygiene & disease prevention

> **Disclaimer:** General field-health education. Not a substitute for medical care.

Historically, **disease and non-battle injury have caused more casualties than combat**.
In the field, hygiene is a survival discipline: it keeps a team functional. Most field
illness is preventable with water discipline, sanitation, foot/hand care, and rest.

## Water-borne illness — the top threat

- Treat all field water (boil 1 min / filter 0.1–0.2 µm / chemical) — see `food-water`.
- Keep "dirty" and "clean" water clearly separated; never let untreated water touch
  your mouth, cookware, or wound care.
- Diarrheal illness causes dehydration that compounds every other problem — prevent it.

## Hand & food hygiene

- Wash or sanitize hands before eating and after latrine use — the single highest-yield
  habit.
- Keep food covered and rations sealed; rotate stock; avoid spoilage.

## Human waste & camp sanitation

- Site latrines/cat-holes **downhill and well away** from water sources and sleeping
  areas; cover waste.
- Keep the rest area clean and free of food scraps (also reduces signature and pests).

## Foot care — mission-critical

- Keep feet dry; change into dry socks (wool/synthetic, not cotton); air feet at halts.
- Manage hot spots **before** they blister (tape/moleskin); treat and protect blisters.
- Watch for **trench foot / immersion foot** in wet cold — a recurring casualty in
  recent conflicts; prevention is dry feet and rotation.

## Skin, teeth, and the small things

- Treat minor cuts/abrasions early to prevent infection; keep wounds clean and covered.
- Brush teeth — dental problems are debilitating and hard to fix in the field.
- Manage chafing; address sleep and nutrition (run-down bodies get sick).

## Vector-borne & environmental

- Reduce insect exposure (cover up, repellent, treated clothing) — mosquitoes/ticks
  carry serious disease.
- Manage heat and cold injury proactively (see `environment`).
