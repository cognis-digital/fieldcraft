# Night & limited visibility

> **Disclaimer:** Educational/defensive awareness of operating in darkness and poor
> visibility. Not operational tactics for attacking anyone.

Darkness and poor visibility (fog, smoke, heavy weather) are double-edged: they
conceal you, but they also degrade your own movement, navigation, and medical care.
Modern sensors (thermal, image intensification) reduce the cover of darkness for
those who have them — and reveal those who don't manage signature.

## Your night vision (the free kind)

- **Dark adaptation** takes ~20–30 minutes and is destroyed in a second by white
  light. Protect it: use the dimmest light possible, ideally red/green, cupped and
  brief. One careless headlamp resets everyone's eyes and marks your position.
- **Off-center viewing** — at night the center of your vision is weak; look slightly
  *beside* an object to see it (the rods are off-axis).
- **Scanning** — move your eyes in slow, deliberate steps and pause; sweeping misses
  detail.

## Devices (what they are)

- **Image intensification (NVGs)** amplify ambient light — they need *some* light and
  are defeated by total darkness or washed out by bright sources.
- **Thermal** sees heat regardless of light — it sees through darkness and light
  camouflage, which is why heat-signature management (see `camouflage-concealment`)
  matters day and night.
- Both are increasingly common; assume a capable observer may have them and manage
  light, heat, and movement accordingly.

## Moving at limited visibility

- Slow down; shorten your pace count expectations and navigate by terrain + compass
  bearing (GPS may be denied — see `lessons`).
- Light and noise discipline is absolute: no white light, phones shielded, gear
  taped/silenced.
- Use a buddy system and a simple, rehearsed signal plan; it's easy to separate in
  the dark.
- Identify rally points and head-counts; account for everyone at every halt.

## Weather & cold-at-night

Temperatures drop and casualties from cold rise at night. Pre-stage warm layers,
keep moving moderately or shelter, and watch teammates for early hypothermia
(shivering, clumsiness, confusion — see `environment` and `medical`).
