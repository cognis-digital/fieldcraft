# Tactical fitness & human performance

> **Disclaimer:** General physical-readiness education. Consult a physician before
> starting a program; train progressively to avoid injury.

The "tactical athlete" must be ready for unscheduled, mixed demands: carry heavy
loads over distance, move explosively in short bursts, work while sleep-deprived and
underfed, and *do it without getting injured*. The biggest readiness threat is not a
lack of peak strength — it's preventable musculoskeletal injury and burnout.

## The pillars

- **Aerobic base** — most field work is long, moderate effort. Build a deep aerobic
  base (zone-2 running/rucking/cycling). This is what lets you keep going for hours
  and recover between efforts.
- **Strength** — full-body, compound movements (squat, hinge/deadlift, press, pull,
  carry). Strength is armor against injury and the engine for load-carriage.
- **Work capacity / anaerobic** — intervals and circuits for the short, intense bursts.
- **Load carriage (rucking)** — train it specifically and progressively (see `rucking`);
  it's a distinct skill and a top injury source if rushed.
- **Mobility & durability** — ankles, hips, shoulders, and the posterior chain;
  prehab the common failure points (knees, low back, feet).

## Programming principles

- **Progressive overload, then deload** — add load/volume gradually (~10%/week guide),
  and schedule lighter recovery weeks.
- **Specificity** — train how you'll perform: rucking under load, carries, get-ups,
  uneven terrain — not just gym lifts.
- **Concurrent training** — balance strength and endurance; don't let one crowd out
  the other.
- **Recover on purpose** — sleep is the #1 performance multiplier; protect it.

## Sustained operations & underfueled performance

- Expect degraded cognition and strength when sleep-deprived and underfed — plan for
  it (simplify decisions, use checklists, buddy-check).
- Fuel: prioritize calories, carbohydrate for hard work, protein for repair, and
  hydration + electrolytes. (See `food-water`.)
- Foot care and blister prevention are fitness-critical in the field (see `rucking`,
  `field-hygiene`).

## Standards to benchmark against (descriptive)

Public military fitness tests (e.g. the U.S. Army Combat Fitness Test) and ruck-march
standards give concrete targets — use them as benchmarks, build toward them
progressively.
