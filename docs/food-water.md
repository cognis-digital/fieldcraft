# Food, water & rations

> **Disclaimer:** Educational / reference / preparedness material with links to public and open-source resources. Not operational instructions for violence or weapons. Verify against primary sources; train with qualified instructors. Follow all applicable laws.

Food, water, and rations knowledge covers the full spectrum of sustaining life in field and emergency conditions — from sourcing and purifying raw water, to calculating caloric needs, selecting and storing rations, and safely supplementing with foraged foods. This reference draws on US Army field manuals, CDC/EPA water guidance, FEMA preparedness standards, and peer-reviewed military nutrition research. It is intended for soldiers, preppers, emergency managers, and civilians building genuine preparedness knowledge. All purification dosages and calorie figures cited are sourced from authoritative public-domain documents.

## Water Requirements and Sourcing

The human body can survive approximately three days without water; cognitive and physical performance degrades significantly after losing just 2% of body weight in fluid. FEMA and Ready.gov recommend storing a minimum of one gallon per person per day, with a planning horizon of at least 14 days (updated guidance). In hot environments or during heavy physical exertion, requirements can double to two gallons per person per day.

Priority water sources in the field, ranked from safest to most suspect:
1. Sealed commercial bottled water or military-issue water
2. Treated municipal tap water stored in clean containers
3. Flowing surface water (streams, springs) — requires purification
4. Standing surface water (ponds, pools) — high contamination risk, requires purification
5. Collected rainwater — generally safer but still not sterile
6. Ground seepage / dew collection — low yield but often low contamination

Do NOT drink: swimming pool or spa water, water from automotive radiators/boilers, floodwater, or any water that may contain fuel or industrial chemicals — chemical contamination cannot be removed by boiling or standard field filters.

Dehydration signs progress through stages: thirst and dark urine (mild, ~2% loss), headache/dizziness/reduced output (moderate, 4-6%), confusion and extreme weakness (severe, >8%). NEVER ration drinking water unless authorities order it. Mild dehydration is corrected by oral rehydration; severe dehydration requires IV fluids in a medical facility.

Hyponatremia (water intoxication) warning: Drinking excessive plain water during prolonged exertion flushes sodium, causing sodium levels to drop dangerously. The US Army limits fluid intake to 1–1.5 liters per hour during heavy activity. Symptoms — headache, nausea, mental-status changes, seizures — can mimic dehydration and have caused deaths in basic trainees. Oral rehydration salts (ORS) or electrolyte solutions prevent this during sustained effort.

## Water Purification Methods

Three categories of contaminants must be addressed: protozoa (Giardia, Cryptosporidium, 5+ microns), bacteria (E. coli, cholera, 0.2–0.5 microns), and viruses (hepatitis A, norovirus, ~0.004 microns). No single field method is perfect against all three without combining approaches.

**Boiling (most reliable)**
Bring clear water to a rolling boil for 1 minute; at elevations above 5,000–6,500 feet, boil for 3 minutes (water boils at lower temperatures at altitude). Kills all pathogens — bacteria, viruses, and protozoa including Cryptosporidium, which resists chemical treatment. Pre-filter cloudy water through clean cloth, coffee filter, or paper towel before boiling. Does not remove chemical pollutants. (Source: CDC, EPA)

**Chlorine bleach (household, unscented, 6–8.25% sodium hypochlorite)**
- Clear water: 8 drops per gallon (6%) or 6 drops per gallon (8.25%)
- Cloudy/cold water: double the dose
- Wait at least 30 minutes; water should have a faint chlorine odor
Effective against bacteria and most viruses. Less effective against Cryptosporidium. (Source: EPA)

**Iodine (2% tincture)**
- Clear water: 5 drops per quart; cloudy: 10 drops per quart
- Wait 30 minutes minimum; in cold water (<40°F/4°C) double contact time
- Works best above 68°F (21°C). More effective than chlorine against Giardia cysts
- Not recommended for pregnant women, those with thyroid conditions, or long-term use

**Chlorine dioxide tablets (e.g., Katadyn MP1)**
Effective against bacteria, viruses, Giardia, and Cryptosporidium — broader spectrum than iodine or plain chlorine. Allow up to 4 hours contact time for Cryptosporidium in cold water. (Source: Princeton OA Guide)

**Portable filtration (mechanical)**
Filter pore sizes determine effectiveness:
- 1.0–4.0 microns: removes protozoa (Giardia, Cryptosporidium)
- 0.2–1.0 microns: also removes bacteria
- 0.004 microns (ultrafilters): removes viruses
Most field filters (e.g., Sawyer Squeeze, Katadyn Hiker) remove protozoa and bacteria but NOT viruses. In regions with poor sanitation or flood conditions, follow filtration with chemical disinfection or UV treatment.

**UV treatment (SteriPen-type devices)**
Effective against bacteria, viruses, and protozoa in clear water. Requires batteries. Ineffective in turbid/cloudy water — pre-filter first.

**SODIS (Solar Disinfection)**
Fill clear PET bottles with turbidity under 30 NTU; place in direct sunlight for 6 hours (sunny) or 48 hours (overcast). Requires solar intensity of at least 500 W/m² for 5 hours. WHO-endorsed low-cost method for emergencies and low-resource settings. Does not work in glass or opaque bottles.

**Best practice**: Filter cloudy water through cloth first, then apply chemical treatment or boiling. The CDC recommends combining filtration and chemical disinfection for the safest result when boiling is not available.

## Military Ration Types and Calorie Specifications

The US military operates a tiered ration system governed by AR 40-25 (Nutrition and Menu Standards for Human Performance Optimization). All rations meet the Military Recommended Daily Allowances (MRDA) of vitamins and minerals across three meals per day.

**Individual Rations**

MRE (Meal, Ready-to-Eat) — the standard individual ration:
- ~1,285 kcal per meal; 40g protein, 176g carbohydrate, 47g fat
- Designed for 3 meals/day (~3,855 kcal total), meeting MRDA
- 24 menus; fully self-contained including flameless ration heater (FRH), utensils, condiments
- Sole-source feeding authorized up to 21 consecutive days
- Shelf life: 3 years at 80°F (27°C); up to 10 years at 60°F (16°C)

First Strike Ration (FSR) — compact assault ration:
- ~2,900 kcal for 24-hour period; 91g protein, 421g carbs, 98g fat
- Designed for high-intensity, short-duration operations (1–3 days); minimal prep
- Lighter and more compact than 3 MREs combined

Meal, Cold Weather (MCW):
- ~1,600 kcal per meal; 3-meal daily ration exceeds 4,500 kcal to offset cold-weather thermogenesis
- All components remain flexible at sub-freezing temperatures

**Group Rations (Unitized Group Rations, UGR)** — each module serves 50 warfighters:
- UGR-A: ~1,270 kcal/meal; frozen components, requires field kitchen, highest quality
- UGR-H&S (Heat and Serve): ~1,370 kcal/meal; shelf-stable, submerge trays in hot water
- UGR-Express (UGR-E): ~1,360 kcal/meal; ready in 30–45 min, no cooks required
- UGR-Marines (UGR-M): ~1,300 kcal/meal; shelf-stable Marine Corps primary group ration

**Survival Rations**
- Survival, General Purpose: ~1,450 kcal; 5 bars + tablets + tea; up to 5 consecutive days
- Survival, Abandon Ship: ~2,400 kcal; 6 bars over 3 days; Navy maritime use
- Survival, Aircraft Life Raft: ~300 kcal; hard candy only; 24-hour minimum

**Modular Operational Rations Enhancement (MORE)**: ~1,000 kcal supplement pack for extreme cold/altitude environments where caloric demand exceeds standard ration output.

Field note: MREs are calorie-dense and high in sodium; they can cause constipation during sustained use. Adequate water intake is essential. A soldier in heavy activity may need 4,000–6,000 kcal/day; standard 3-MRE issue targets the average but may underserve those in extreme cold or sustained operations.

## Civilian Calorie and Ration Planning

**Daily Calorie Baselines (civilian emergency context)**
- Sedentary adult: 1,800–2,200 kcal/day
- Active adult / light labor: 2,500–3,000 kcal/day
- Heavy physical labor, cold environments: 3,000–4,000 kcal/day
- Children 2–8 years: ~1,000–1,400 kcal/day
- Children 9–13: ~1,400–2,000 kcal/day
- Pregnant/nursing women: add ~300–500 kcal above baseline

FEMA's standard recommendation is a minimum 3-day portable supply; updated preparedness guidance suggests 2 weeks for households. More comprehensive preparedness programs recommend 30–90 days.

**Calorie-dense storage staples (per 100g dry weight)**
- White rice: ~365 kcal
- Wheat berries/flour: ~340 kcal
- Dried beans/lentils: ~340 kcal
- Rolled oats: ~389 kcal
- Honey: ~304 kcal (also antimicrobial)
- Peanut butter: ~590 kcal
- Vegetable oil: ~884 kcal (highest caloric density)
- Pasta: ~370 kcal

**Planning formula**: (daily kcal target) × (number of people) × (number of days) = total kcal needed. Divide by the calorie density of your staple to get storage volume.

Example: 4 people, 14 days, 2,500 kcal/person/day = 140,000 kcal needed. At 365 kcal/100g rice, that is approximately 38 kg (84 lbs) of rice for caloric needs alone — supplement with protein, fats, and micronutrient sources.

**Nutritional balance**: Pure calorie storage without micronutrient variety causes deficiency disease (scurvy from lack of vitamin C, pellagra from niacin deficiency). Include multivitamins, canned or freeze-dried vegetables and fruits, and varied protein sources. Salt for preservation and electrolyte replacement is also critical.

## Food Storage: Shelf Life and Best Practices

Shelf life is primarily determined by four factors: temperature, moisture/humidity, oxygen exposure, and light. Every 10°F (5.5°C) increase above optimal storage temperature roughly halves shelf life. Ideal conditions: 40–60°F (4–16°C), low humidity, dark, and oxygen-free.

**Shelf-life reference table (optimal sealed storage)**

| Food | Standard Shelf | Optimal Sealed | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| White rice (polished) | 1–2 yr (open) | 25–30 years (O2 absorber, 40–70°F) | BYU 30-yr study confirms quality retention |
| Wheat berries | ~5 yr (warm) | 25 years (cool basement, O2 free) | Garage storage degrades to ~5 yr |
| Dried beans | 1 yr (open bag) | 10–30 years (#10 can or Mylar + O2 absorber) | 80%+ acceptance at 30 yr in taste panels |
| Rolled oats | ~1 yr (open) | 20–30 years (oxygen-free container) | |
| Pasta | 2–5 yr | 8–10 years (O2 absorber) | |
| Honey (filtered, commercial) | Indefinite | Indefinite | Naturally antimicrobial; may crystallize but remains safe |
| Salt | Indefinite | Indefinite | Non-iodized; iodized loses potency over time |
| White sugar | Indefinite | Indefinite | Keep moisture-free |
| Canned goods (commercial) | 2–5 yr (label) | Often safe beyond 'best-by' | Discard if bulging, dented on seam, or foul-smelling |
| Freeze-dried (commercial) | 15–25 years (sealed) | Up to 25 years at <70°F | Most nutrient-retentive long-storage method |
| MRE (military) | 3 yr (80°F) | Up to 10 yr (<60°F) | Accelerated shelf-life testing data from Natick |
| Cooking oil | 1–4 yr | Best consumed within 2 yr; refrigerate after opening | Rancidity degrades caloric quality |

**Packaging methods** (best to adequate):
1. #10 steel cans with oxygen absorbers — gold standard; impact-resistant, rodent-proof
2. Mylar bags + oxygen absorbers sealed in food-grade buckets — excellent, lower cost
3. PETE/PET plastic bottles with oxygen absorbers — good for grains/beans up to 1 gallon
4. Standard food-grade buckets without O2 absorbers — adequate for rotation stock, shorter term

**Rotation principle**: FIFO (First In, First Out) — always use oldest stock first and replace. Check stores every 6–12 months for moisture, pests, or container failure.

**What NOT to store**: Foods with high fat/oil content (nuts, crackers) go rancid quickly; brown rice contains oils and lasts only 6 months. Avoid glass containers (breakage in earthquakes/disasters).

## Water Storage and Containerization

**Volume planning**: FEMA minimum is 1 gallon/person/day. Upgraded guidance for a 2-week supply for a family of 4 = 4 people × 14 days × 1 gallon = 56 gallons minimum (drinking and sanitation only; add more for hygiene). Hot climates or individuals doing physical labor need 2 gallons/person/day.

**Approved container types**:
- Commercial bottled water (factory-sealed) — simplest option; rotate every 1–2 years
- Food-grade PETE/HDPE containers (blue 5- or 7-gallon barrels, 55-gallon drums) — for bulk home storage
- WaterBOB bathtub bladder — fills standard bathtub with ~100 gallons of clean tap water before a storm; single-use emergency option
- 2-liter PET soda bottles (cleaned) — acceptable short-term; do not use milk jugs (HDPE milk jug plastic degrades and leaks)

**Treating water before storage**:
- Commercial bottled water: no treatment needed if factory-sealed
- Tap water in clean containers: no additional treatment needed if from a treated municipal source; it already contains residual chlorine
- Well water stored at home: treat with bleach (see purification section) before sealing

**Storage conditions**: Keep away from fuel, chemicals, and direct sunlight. Avoid temperature extremes. Do not store near concrete floors (leaching concern with some plastics — use pallets or shelving).

**Rotation**: Replace stored tap water every 6–12 months; commercial bottled water every 1–2 years.

**Emergency improvised sources**: Hot water heaters (hold 30–80 gallons of potable water accessible via drain valve), toilet tank water (not bowl), pipes — drain by opening a low faucet after cutting the main supply. All should be treated before drinking after a contamination event.

## Foraging: Safety Principles and Caveats

Foraging can supplement rations but carries serious and potentially fatal risks. It should be treated as a supplementary skill with strict safety discipline — not a primary food strategy without substantial prior training.

**Core safety rules**
1. Never eat any plant unless you are 100% certain of identification. Use at minimum two to three field guides and cross-reference with an expert.
2. One trait is never enough — identify by the combination of leaf shape, stem cross-section, root structure, smell, habitat, and season.
3. Do NOT assume animal behavior indicates safety for humans. Birds and deer eat many plants that are toxic to people (e.g., nightshade berries).
4. Avoid foraging near roads, railway lines, power line corridors, agricultural fields, or industrial zones — these areas accumulate herbicides, pesticides, heavy metals, and fuel residue in plant tissue.
5. Taste and smell are NOT reliable safety indicators. Poisonous plants can taste mild or smell pleasant.
6. Know your region — a species that is safe in one region may have a toxic look-alike in another.

**High-danger look-alikes (common)**
- Wild carrot (Queen Anne's Lace) vs. Poison hemlock and Water hemlock — both deadly; hemlock has purple mottling on stems and lacks the carrot's root smell
- Wild onion/garlic vs. Death camas — death camas has no onion odor; any 'onion' without the smell is suspect
- Morel mushroom vs. False morel (Gyromitra) — false morals have a brain-like cap, not a true honeycomb; some species are fatal
- Elderberries (toxic raw, safe cooked) vs. Pokeweed (all parts toxic)
- Blackberries/raspberries vs. Nightshade berries — always check leaf and stem structure

**Universal Edibility Test (Army FM 21-76)**
The US Army Survival Manual FM 21-76 describes a multi-step plant edibility test for true survival emergencies when no other food is available and positive identification is impossible. The process involves skin contact, lip/tongue contact in sequence, with 8-hour waiting periods between each step to detect reactions before consuming. This test does NOT work for mushrooms, cannot detect some toxins (e.g., cyanogenic glycosides in raw elderberries), and is a last resort only — it is time-consuming and not foolproof.

**Generally safer beginner plants** (with positive ID confirmed, in pristine areas):
- Dandelion (Taraxacum officinale) — all parts edible; leaves, flowers, roasted root
- Cattail (Typha spp.) — pollen, shoots, and root starch edible at various seasons
- Purslane (Portulaca oleracea) — succulent leaves, good omega-3 content
- Clovers (Trifolium spp.) — flowers and leaves edible raw or cooked
- Plantain (Plantago major) — not the banana; leaf is edible raw or cooked

If you suspect plant poisoning: call Poison Control (US: 1-800-222-1222) or 911 immediately. Do not wait for symptoms to worsen.

## Field Hydration and Nutrition Under Stress

**Hydration in the field**
The US Army's programmed drinking guidance limits intake to 1–1.5 liters per hour during heavy activity to balance hydration against hyponatremia risk. In extreme heat (WBGT above 90°F), a resting soldier may still need 1 liter/hour. Electrolyte replacement (sodium, potassium, magnesium) becomes critical after the first hour of heavy sweating — plain water alone is insufficient for extended exertion.

Oral Rehydration Solution (ORS, WHO formula): 1 liter water + 6 teaspoons sugar + 0.5 teaspoon salt. This can be improvised from field supplies. Commercial options include Pedialyte, Liquid IV, or military Combat Casualty Care electrolyte packets.

**Signs of dehydration in the field**:
- Mild (1–2%): thirst, darker urine, slight performance decrease
- Moderate (3–5%): headache, dry mouth, reduced concentration, cramps
- Severe (>6%): confusion, rapid heart rate, sunken eyes, very dark or no urine
- Emergency (>10%): delirium, loss of consciousness — requires IV fluids

**Nutrition under stress and cold**
Metabolic rate rises 7–10% per degree Celsius drop in core body temperature. A soldier or survivor in sub-freezing conditions may need 4,000–6,000 kcal/day to maintain body temperature without shivering. Fat is the most calorie-dense fuel (9 kcal/g vs. 4 kcal/g for carbs/protein) and is preferred for cold-weather sustained work; the MCW ration reflects this with elevated fat content.

Carbohydrates provide rapid energy for burst activity; protein is critical for tissue repair after injury or sustained physical stress. The military's Modular Operational Rations Enhancement (MORE) pack adds ~1,000 kcal of supplement for extreme-environment shortfalls.

**Food and water interaction**: High-sodium foods (MREs, jerky, crackers) increase water demand. In water-scarce situations, avoid high-protein, high-salt rations and prioritize carbohydrate-heavy foods (rice, pasta, bread) which require less water to metabolize. Alcohol and caffeine both increase urinary water loss — avoid or minimize in water-scarce conditions.

**Food safety in the field**: Cooked food left at room temperature (40–140°F danger zone) should not exceed 2 hours before consumption. Never eat from bulging or dented-seam cans. Any ration with off-odor, discoloration, or unusual texture should be discarded.

### Checklist: 72-Hour Emergency Water Kit (per person)

- [ ] 3 gallons of water minimum stored in food-grade HDPE or PETE containers
- [ ] Unscented household bleach (5–8.25% sodium hypochlorite) with dosage card
- [ ] Eyedropper or measuring dropper for bleach dosing
- [ ] Portable water filter rated to at least 0.2 microns (removes bacteria and protozoa)
- [ ] Chemical disinfection tablets (chlorine dioxide preferred for broadest spectrum)
- [ ] Collapsible or rigid water containers for collection if resupply needed
- [ ] Coffee filters or clean cloth for pre-filtering turbid water
- [ ] Water purification reference card (boil times, bleach doses)

### Checklist: 14-Day Food Storage Checklist (1 adult, ~2,500 kcal/day)

- [ ] ~10 lbs white rice (~3,640 kcal, stored in sealed Mylar bag with O2 absorber)
- [ ] ~5 lbs dried beans or lentils (~2,720 kcal, protein source)
- [ ] ~3 lbs rolled oats (~5,300 kcal, breakfast staple)
- [ ] ~2 lbs pasta (~3,000 kcal)
- [ ] 16 oz peanut butter or nut butter (~2,700 kcal, fat and protein)
- [ ] 16 oz honey (calories + natural preservative)
- [ ] Multivitamin supply for 14 days (prevents micronutrient deficiency)
- [ ] Salt (electrolyte; also food preservation)
- [ ] Canned vegetables and fruit (variety; vitamin C source)
- [ ] Canned protein (tuna, sardines, chicken — 6–10 cans)
- [ ] Cooking oil (vegetable or olive; 1 quart minimum for calorie density)
- [ ] Manual can opener
- [ ] Portable stove or camp stove with fuel for 14 days
- [ ] Waterproof matches or lighter

### Checklist: Water Purification Decision Tree (field use)

- [ ] STEP 1: Is the water visibly turbid or cloudy? If yes, pre-filter through cloth or coffee filter first
- [ ] STEP 2: Do you have fuel and a vessel? Boil 1 minute (3 min above 6,500 ft) — most reliable method
- [ ] STEP 3: No fire available? Use chlorine dioxide tablets if available (broadest spectrum, works on Cryptosporidium with 4-hr wait)
- [ ] STEP 4: No tablets? Use bleach — 8 drops per gallon (clear water), 16 drops (cloudy); wait 30 min
- [ ] STEP 5: No bleach? Use iodine — 5 drops per quart clear, 10 drops cloudy; wait 30 min (60 min if cold)
- [ ] STEP 6: Portable filter only? Add chemical treatment after filtering — most field filters do NOT remove viruses
- [ ] STEP 7: No chemical, no fire? SODIS — clear PET bottle, <30 NTU turbidity, full sun 6 hours or 48 hours overcast
- [ ] STEP 8: NEVER use floodwater with possible chemical contamination — use only bottled water

### Checklist: Foraging Safety Pre-Checklist

- [ ] Carry at least two regional field guides and cross-reference before eating anything
- [ ] Verify identification using multiple plant characteristics (leaf shape, stem, root, smell, habitat, season)
- [ ] Learn the top 5–10 toxic species in your specific region before foraging
- [ ] Do NOT forage within 50 meters of roads, railroads, or agricultural fields (contamination)
- [ ] Do NOT assume safety because wildlife or livestock eat the plant
- [ ] Do NOT rely on taste or smell alone — some deadly plants are mild or pleasant-tasting
- [ ] Prepare foraged food correctly — some safe species require cooking (elderberries, cattail pollen, nettles)
- [ ] If in doubt, do not eat it — caloric deficit for 1–2 days is safer than plant poisoning
- [ ] Have Poison Control number accessible: US 1-800-222-1222

### Checklist: Dehydration Self-Assessment Field Checklist

- [ ] Check urine color: pale yellow = well hydrated; dark yellow/amber = mild to moderate dehydration; no output = severe
- [ ] Mild symptoms (thirst, headache, fatigue): increase fluid intake, add electrolytes if available
- [ ] Moderate symptoms (dizziness, confusion, rapid heart rate): cease heavy activity, rest in shade, ORS solution
- [ ] Do NOT attempt to 'catch up' rapidly — drink steadily 250–500 mL at a time
- [ ] Monitor for hyponatremia if drinking large volumes of plain water during prolonged exertion: symptoms include nausea, headache, confusion, seizure — add electrolytes, do NOT give more plain water
- [ ] Severe dehydration with altered mental status requires evacuation and IV fluids — field first aid is supportive only
